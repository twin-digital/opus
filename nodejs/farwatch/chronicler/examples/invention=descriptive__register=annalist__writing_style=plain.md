They went in through the drainage sluice on the north face of the causeway, three hours before dawn, when the marsh-wardens were tending their fire and keeping their backs to the dark. The water was cold enough to slow a man's hands, but they moved without light and without sound, and the wardens never turned. By the time the outer gate was behind them, the company was through and dripping in the passage beyond with nothing worse than cold feet and the way home still open.

The inner vault was not unguarded. Something had been set there — an old arrangement, a thing of sinew and patience that had waited in the dark a long while. They fought it by torchlight in the low passage, and it was a hard press: the thing turned aside iron as though iron meant nothing, until one of the company thought to try the torch itself. Fire served where blades had not. They backed the thing against the far wall and kept the flame on it until it stopped, and then they rested a moment in the smell of it and pressed on.

The door at the end of the vault's deepest run was what stopped them.

Old stone, fitted close, with markings cut into the lintel and frame in a hand none of them knew. Two in the company had read older scripts than most men ever encounter, and they worked at those carvings for better than an hour by guttering torchlight. The marks gave nothing back. Whether the script was a branch of the old teaching that had gone a different way, or something that predated the tradition entirely, they could not say — but in the end they could not determine whether what was written there was a ward, a mechanism, a name, or a warning. They left the question unanswered because they had no other choice.

Then they put their shoulders to the door. Three at first, braced in the passage; then all of them together, heels dug into the stone floor. The door did not shift, did not grind, did not give the least indication that it could be persuaded by weight. Either it moved by means of the markings, and the markings were still dark to them, or something on the far side held it that they had no way to reach.

They came back out through the sluice as the sky was beginning to grey. The wardens were changing over. The company made the marsh path before full light.

The vault's contents remain where they were.

The company set out in the grey close of autumn, bound through the fenlands for the drowned section of the old causeway where, by reliable account, a sealed vault had stood undisturbed beneath the floodwater for three generations.

The fenlands had keepers — marsh-wardens whose hereditary claim the crown had long since stopped contesting. They did not look kindly on outsiders pressing past their gate-posts without leave. The company's leader chose to meet them honestly rather than attempt a crossing under cover and invite a lasting feud. She called for a parley at the warden-station on the eastern approach and laid out the company's purpose plainly: survey work, a sealed vault, nothing taken without accounting. The head warden was a patient antagonist; he tested her word the way men do when they have watched too many reckless parties come through already, asking the same questions in different arrangements and watching her answers for cracks that never appeared. By the time the light failed, he had satisfied himself, and he gave his leave — along with the name of a ferrywoman who knew which crossings held and which had been swallowed.

The ferrywoman held a monopoly on the inner fenways and priced accordingly. The toll she quoted for a full company with gear was steep enough to draw a breath. The company paid it without negotiation, partly because there was no other crossing and partly because they had come prepared for exactly this: the fenlands ran on river-silver rather than goodwill, and they had provisioned for the market as it stood. What the payment bought them was three days on her flat-bottomed barge, her knowledge of the channels, and her studied disinterest in what they were after — which proved worth more than the passage itself.

What coin could not purchase was the vault's final approach. A long section of the old causeway lay submerged, accessible only on foot, and the water in late autumn ran cold enough to make a man's hands useless before he had worked half his length of rope. They ran lines from the exposed stonework above the waterline and went in by pairs. Two of the company cramped badly and had to be helped back to the bank; the rest pressed on, the cold working steadily at everyone who remained in the water. It took the better part of a morning, twice the reckoning they had made on dry land. They endured it. When the last of them hauled up onto the stone floor of the vault's interior and sat in the dark waiting for the feeling to return to their hands, the way behind them still held — and before them the vault lay open and untouched.

The company set out for the drowned vault at the edge of the eastern fens in the last week of autumn, when the water was low enough to find footing on the old causeway. Three days' travel through boggy ground brought them to the site before sundown on the fourth.

The wardens of the place were known to them, at least by report. The marsh-folk said they moved at dusk around the outer ring of flooded stones, and that quiet passage was possible if a party went carefully before full dark. So the company stripped their lanterns, bound their gear tight, and made their way in two by two along the stones, keeping low.

They were seen before they reached the inner arch.

How it happened is a matter of some disagreement among those who returned. What is settled is that the shapes came up from the water on three sides, moving without sound, and that slipping back out the way they had come was no longer a choice the company held. They stood caught on the wrong side of the channel, in open ground, with the vault ahead and the wardens between them and the way home.

What followed came from the eldest of the party, who had dealt with such things before. She told the others to set down their packs and be still. Then she unpacked the sealed vessel she had carried from the covenant house — a thing of some worth, as the covenant reckoned worth — and opened it at the water's edge and let the contents run into the black water. No word was spoken. The wardens held where they stood, and after a long moment, drew back.

The company retrieved what they had come for and were out before dawn.
