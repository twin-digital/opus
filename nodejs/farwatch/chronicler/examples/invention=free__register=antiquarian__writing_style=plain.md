# The Vetharn Underhalls: An Account of the Expedition in the Thirty-First Year

The party the compact sent to Vetharn in the frost-month returned without what it went to find. The record of that expedition deserves a plain telling, for the failure was not the fault of the men and women involved, nor of poor judgment, but of a problem that turned out to be older and harder than anyone had reckoned.

---

The object of the expedition was the Registrar's archive — a cache of bound records believed to have been sealed into the underhalls at Vetharn when the Siltwood Compact's founders abandoned the site some eighty years before. The compact's interest was a title dispute of long standing, one that the archive, if its contents proved to be what the older members insisted they were, might at last have settled.

The mound at Vetharn was still watched. The Vaunt — a merchant faction with its own claim on the records — had stationed outriders on the surface, men who made a circuit of the earthworks at irregular intervals to discourage precisely the sort of visit the compact intended. The party's warden, a woman called Orveth, observed one full circuit from the tree-line before she moved. She had identified the charcoal-cutter's shaft on the mound's eastern face, an entry the Vaunt's men did not watch because they did not know it was there, and she brought the party down through it one at a time in the window between two passes. By the time the outriders came around again, the rope was already drawn up and the shaft was empty.

Deeper in, the underhalls were not as deserted as the compact had hoped. A company of hired blades had taken up the antechamber the old maps called the Hall of Scales — camped there for reasons the party could not determine, and unwilling to be removed by discussion. There was a fight. It was short and decided in the party's favor: the Vaunt's men broke and retreated down a side-passage, and the Hall of Scales belonged to the expedition. They pressed on.

The archive lay close. But between the antechamber and it stood the Registrar's own sealing-work: two bronze doors set into the living rock, covered from floor to lintel in close-cut script. The party's scholar, a man named Pessel who had been retained for his knowledge of Compact-era documentation, set to work on the inscription and did not stop until he had exhausted himself against it. The script was not Compact-era. It was older — a tradition the Compact's founders had themselves inherited and half-transformed, and which Pessel knew only by its shape, not its substance. He recognized the lineage of the characters but could not recover the particular sealing-phrase buried in them. He told Orveth he did not know, which was the honest answer. Orveth accepted it.

The doors were bronze, but they had been mounted with the deliberateness of builders who meant them to stand indefinitely. Two of the party put their shoulders to the work for the better part of an hour. The doors did not shift. The hinge-plates were sunk so far into the dressed stone that the iron bar brought for such contingencies bent against the frame before the frame so much as creaked, and Orveth called off the attempt before a second bar followed the first.

The party withdrew the way it had come: past the Hall of Scales, up the charcoal-cutter's shaft, out onto the frost-stiff grass of the mound. The archive remained sealed. The title dispute remained unsettled. The Vaunt's outriders, pacing their circuits above, never knew how close the matter had come.

The compact sent a party into the Valdric Marches in the last week before the high passes were expected to close — five persons charged with reopening a right of passage that had stood dormant since the breaking of the old compact some twelve years prior.

At Thornmere Ford, a captain named Osric Veldt had held authority over the crossing under a warlord's charter long enough to consider himself its permanent master. The garrison on the high bank numbered enough to make force an expensive argument, so the party's speaker — a woman named Caela, twice the compact's envoy to the southern city councils — sought a proper audience at the ford's counting-house instead. She spent the better part of an afternoon there, laying out the compact's purposes plainly and tracing a chain of obligation that tied Veldt's charter to a lord who owed the compact a debt of standing. Veldt was not a man who ignored a creditor, and before the candles had burned to their midpoints he had written out a pass.

The road beyond Thornmere climbed into the Reaches proper, and at the waystation of Greel's Notch the party found a factor of the Merchant Syndicate of the Inner Passes — a man called Dunston, who held the only reliable guide-service through the unmarked high country. Dunston had no interest in charters or obligations. His ledger spoke only in coin, and his price was steep. The party had brought silver against exactly this contingency and spent it without contest, judging that to haggle was to invite delays the season would not permit. Dunston's guide — a weathered woman who offered no name beyond her trade-sign — met them at dawn and brought them through three days of high passes without error.

She turned back at the edge of the Pale Flats, her contract fulfilled. The Flats are two days of open highland where the wind comes without break from the northwest; they had turned back prior expeditions, and on the second night of this one the cold reached a depth that bit through wool and leather alike. There is no art to such a crossing. The record shows only that when the second morning came, the party rose from ground that had not kept warmth and continued across ground that offered none. On the afternoon of the second day they descended into the sheltered valley where their purpose lay, and what they had been sent to accomplish, they accomplished.

**Of the Company of the Grey Seal, and their passage through the Thresh-Hollow**

The company entered the Thresh-Hollow in the grey hours before dawn, having judged that silence and shadow would carry them through what argument could not. The guardian of those narrows — the thing the valley-folk called the Warden of Knots — was known to exact toll of any who passed openly; but the accounts of older expeditions recorded a deer-track beneath the southern ridgeline, well below the Warden's customary stations. Trusting those accounts, the company resolved to give the Warden no occasion to be met.

The accounts were old. The Warden had extended its reckoning since they were set down. The company was a third of the way along the track when the knotted silence of the place tightened around them — a pressure in the ears, a stilling of wind — and the path ahead was closed by something that had no clear form but filled the passage as a stopper fills a throat. The evasion had come to nothing; they were held.

What followed was not deliberation. The company's eldest, a woman who had passed through such negotiations before, stepped to the front of the party and drew from her pack a bundle wrapped in river-linen — a loom-book of patterns she had spent the better part of two winters compiling, a work she had not intended to part with. She set it on the ground between herself and the Warden without ceremony, and spoke a phrase in a tongue the chronicles preserve but do not translate.

The knots loosened. The pressure eased. The path opened before them.

They came out of the Hollow by midmorning and completed their purposes in the valley beyond before the light failed. The loom-book did not return with them. On the homeward road the Hollow let them through without incident, which the company took as confirmation that the account had been settled in full.
