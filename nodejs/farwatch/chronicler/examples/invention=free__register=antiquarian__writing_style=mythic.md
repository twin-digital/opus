# The Third Venture into the Ossuary of Kael Vor

The expedition — the third mounted by the Compact of the Pale Ledger against that sunken place — departed in the grey hour before dawn, when the watchers posted along the outer ridge would be half-blind and dulled by cold. What follows is set down from the testimony of those who returned, and from the notes kept by the expedition's lore-keeper in the margins of his field-codex.

They came to the ossuary's outer ward by the southern route, skirting the embankments through stands of cedar so dense the party moved in single file, their blades bound in cloth to keep them from ringing, their lanterns shuttered to the last sliver of light. At the breach in the outer wall, three sentinels sat gathered around a fire and spoke of their relief rotation. The party passed beneath the crumbled lintel not ten yards from that fire, moving in a long unhurried line with the wind in their favour, and were not seen. So the first great obstacle of the venture resolved: not by force or argument, but by the older and quieter art of becoming, for a few necessary minutes, less present than the dark.

The inner antechamber was less obliging. A guardian occupied the approach to the main corridor — some custodian of an elder compact, neither wholly of the living nor wholly departed from it — and in so narrow a place there was nothing to do but meet it. Sera Nault held the left flank, keeping the thing's longer arm occupied while the brothers Cormec drove the centre hard. Aldric Vane, the expedition's keeper of lore, remained behind the line but directed the others to press the custodian's left side, where its reach fell short. The thing was stilled — such creatures do not easily perish, but they can be brought to stillness — and the corridor beyond opened at last before them.

Here is where the shape of the venture began to turn.

At the corridor's end stood a door of black-veined stone, and across its full face a warding seal had been inscribed — not one seal, the record makes clear, but many, layered one upon another across what appeared to be several generations of wardens, each hand slightly different from the last, each adding its own grammar of signs to those already present. Vane brought his lamp close and spent the better part of an hour working from the inscription's left edge to its right. He was a man who had read the sealing-scripts of four distinct traditions; what confronted him on that door exceeded four. He named three of the seven sigil-families he identified; the remaining four he could not place in any lineage he knew. He could not determine whether the seal warned, or invoked, or merely identified. He could not say what it required in order to open, nor what it would do if its conditions were violated, nor even whether it was meant to be read in the proper sense or spoken aloud in a tongue he did not possess. He stepped back at last with nothing resolved.

It was Cormec the elder who, when Vane withdrew from the door, concluded that what scholarship could not unfasten, weight and leverage might. Stone can be broken. He examined the frame and traced the lintel's seam, determining that the mortar filling the gap between door and wall was newer than the wall itself and might therefore be persuaded. Three of the party drove a pry-bar into the upper right seam and threw their combined weight behind it. The bar bent. They seated a second bar alongside the first and tried again, and then again; on the third attempt the first bar snapped, and they stood before a door bearing no mark of their efforts and holding two fewer tools than they had carried in.

They were not, by that hour, a party with room to persist. The lamp oil was running low; the stilled custodian would not remain so past the turning of the night; and they did not know what lay beyond the sealed door — whether it would prove worth a fourth venture, a fifth, or any number at all. They withdrew in good order through the antechamber and back through the cedar-darkness of the southern embankments, arriving at the compact's muster-point before first light, having passed unseen by the same sentinels who had not seen them enter.

The compact's ledger records the expedition as a failure, and the ledger is correct. The outer ward was breached; the custodian was stilled; the second chamber of Kael Vor keeps its seal nonetheless, and whatever lies behind that door remains where it was before the party came — patient, as sealed things are, and no more yielding for having been tried.

# Of the Crossing to Valdenmere, and the Compact Restored

In the third year of the Interregnum, when the old roads east of the Ashford passes had grown uncertain and the compact's warrant ran thin beyond the river, a party was dispatched to the Hall of Vestiges at Valdenmere — that fortified archive where the compact between the compact and the Thornwarden houses had been struck a century prior, and where, it was held, a renewal of that compact still awaited ratification. The journey was not reckoned dangerous in the manner of blades and open ground; its dangers, as the account makes plain, were of a subtler kind.

The first obstruction met them at the Greygate crossing, where the road narrows between two collapsed towers and a customs house still flies the banner of a lord three generations dead. In truth the house's authority had passed by inheritance to a collateral line — the Edric Woldermaines, lesser nobility grown territorial in the vacuum the Interregnum had opened — and it was their representative, a sharp-featured factor named Cossic Woldermaine, who rode out to bar the party's passage and declared the road a private toll-way under disputed charter. Another company might have pressed the issue with steel, or turned back to seek another route. This party did neither. Their speaker — a woman of the compact named Isaura Drent, senior of the delegation — dismounted unhurriedly, asked for a table and water for both sides, and then proceeded to speak for the better part of an afternoon.

What Drent offered was not coin, not yet, but standing. She laid out the compact's history with the Thornwarden houses in full, named the Woldermaines' own ancestor as signatory to the original compact, and made plain that the road to Valdenmere was not merely a road but a thread in a weave of obligation that had outlasted three wars. Cossic Woldermaine was not a fool; he understood that to bar the compact's delegation was to declare a rupture that his own house could ill afford. By dusk the gate stood open, and the party rode through on the strength of words alone.

The second trial came not from an enemy but from the archive itself. Valdenmere's custodian, an aged presbyter of the old rites named Tomald Esh, had tended the Hall of Vestiges through the entirety of the Interregnum and had grown, in that isolation, into a precise and formidable guardian of his own authority. The compact's renewal required his formal witnessing; he would not witness it, he informed the delegation, without the deposit of the archival tithe that the compact had allowed to lapse during the wars. The sum he named was not punitive, but it was not small, and it was payable only in the old weight-silver that the Interregnum had made scarce.

Drent sent two riders back to the nearest compact house, and waited. Three days later they returned with a locked chest. Esh counted the weight himself, in silence, and then set his seal to the document with the satisfied gravity of a man whose principles have been, at last, correctly acknowledged. The compact paid what was owed, and in paying acknowledged that the lapse had been theirs.

The final impediment was of a different character than the first two, and it is the one the account dwells on least — which is perhaps why it is the one most worth remarking. The compact's renewal required the delegation to remain at Valdenmere through the Night of Witnesses, that old observance by which parties to a compact are held to have submitted themselves to its terms in full understanding. The night is long in that hall; the fire is kept deliberately low; and the rite requires that those present speak, in turn, of what the compact costs them — not what they gain, but what they surrender. It is a rite designed, one suspects, to find the place where resolve thins.

There is no record of what each member of the delegation said in that hall. That is as it should be; the speaking belongs to the rite, not to the chronicle. What is recorded is that every member of the delegation remained through the full observance, spoke when their turn came, and affixed their mark to the compact at dawn. Tomald Esh, who had witnessed many such nights, noted afterward that he had seen delegations of twice the number break before the fire died down. This one did not.

They rode home with the compact sealed and the Greygate road confirmed open, and the thing that had been fraying for a generation was, for a time, made whole.

# Of the Expedition to the Hollow of Ashen Stones, and What Was Given There

The compact's records for this undertaking name three who set out, though the Hollow remembers only what it chose to keep: the passage of footsteps that were not quiet enough, and the thing that was left behind.

The approach the party took was, by all accounting, a reasonable one. The Hollow of Ashen Stones sits at the foot of the Creel Escarpment in a cup of dead ground where sound collects and the light arrives late. Its guardian — the Warden-That-Walks-No-Path, the old records call it, the compact's own scholars having long declined to invent a better name — does not pursue. It does not give chase. It stands, and it attends, and what it attends to, it marks. The party understood this much. Their intention was therefore to give it nothing to mark: to slip through the outer ring of stones during the grey interval between the last of the night and the first colour of dawn, when the Warden is known to stand at its deepest post and face the escarpment wall.

They came to the outer ring at the appointed hour. That much went as planned.

What the party had not accounted for — what no account in the compact's library had troubled to set down, and what constitutes a notable gap in the record that this chronicler cannot wholly repair — was the litter of old ash that lies loose across the stone floor of the outer ring, banked in drifts by the prevailing wind off the Creel. One of the three disturbed such a drift; the sound it made was not large, as sounds go. But the Hollow is, as noted, a place where sound collects. The grey interval collapsed around them. The Warden turned from the escarpment wall, and the party found themselves stood in the full, unblinking regard of something that had been attending to that place since before the compact had a name.

They might have run. They did not run. Whether they judged it futile, or whether something in the quality of that attention made flight feel like a small and foolish thing to attempt, the chronicler cannot say with certainty — but the records of their character incline toward the former, and the compact's wiser veterans toward the latter. They stood their ground.

What happened then is the difficult portion to relate, because the party's own account is imprecise in a way that suggests the event did not leave precise impressions. What is clear is this: Seren Vaulk, the eldest of the three, stepped forward from the others and placed upon the flat altar-stone at the ring's centre the thing she had carried since her induction into the compact — a talisman of undocumented provenance, sealed in wax, never opened, the nature of its contents left for her to determine alone, as is the custom. She had carried it for eleven years without opening it. She placed it down, and she stepped back, and she did not reach for it again.

The Warden's regard shifted. Not away — it is not recorded that the Warden ever looks away — but it shifted in quality, as a scale shifts when weight is added to one pan. The party moved through the Hollow. The ash did not stir. The stones did not ring. Whatever the inner sanctum held, they reached it; whatever the expedition required of that place, it was accomplished; and they came out again into a morning that had by then gone fully bright, blinking in a light that fell on them as it falls on anyone, indifferently.

Seren Vaulk came home with the rest. She did not speak of the talisman on the road back, nor in the testimony she gave upon returning, nor — so far as any record shows — at any point thereafter. The compact does not press its members on such matters. The custom exists because the contents are meant to matter only to the one who carries them, and the compact's tradition holds that what is given in the Hollow is given completely, which is to say: it is not a trade. It is not a bargain struck. It is only a door that opened, and a willingness to pay the cost of that.

The expedition was counted a success. This chronicle notes, without further comment, that Seren Vaulk thereafter carried nothing at her belt where the talisman had hung, and that she never explained what it had contained, and that she never seemed inclined to mourn it.
