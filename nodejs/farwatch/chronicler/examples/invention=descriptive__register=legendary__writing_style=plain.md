# The Warden's Vault

In the third season of that year's watch, when the lowland mists lay thick and the marsh-paths were known only to those born to them, a party went out from the compact to recover what had been sealed in the old wardens' keep at the edge of the causeways. The keep had stood a generation without garrison, its doors mired in reed and silt, and what lay within had been a matter of dispute among the elders long enough that the elders had finally resolved to send someone to look.

They crossed the outer earthworks in the grey before dawn, moving low through the reed-beds with their cloaks dragging in the black water. Two watchmen held the causeway where it narrowed to a single plank-width over the drowned ditch — but the party went by water rather than timber, chest-deep and soundless, and the watchmen never looked down. They reached the keep's sunken entry before the mist had lifted.

Inside, they found the keep was not empty. A small company had sheltered there — marsh-folk of no compact, who had made the ruin their own and did not welcome the intrusion. The party did not try to parley; they understood at a glance that parley would cost them the surprise they had bought at such cold effort. The fight was close and loud, and it ended with the marsh-folk scattered into the reed-passages and the compact's people in possession of the hall.

In the center of that hall stood the vault door they had come for — old iron set into the stone below the waterline, its face worked over with inscription. The party's lorekeeper set to it at once, tracing the letters with a wet finger, moving from line to line. But the inscription was older than she had reckoned, and the dialect it used had shifted beneath her learning in ways she could not compensate for on the ground, in the dark, with the marsh water rising around her boots. She read what she could. She was certain of the first clause and the last, and she reasoned the middle by inference. She was wrong, though she did not know it yet.

Acting on her reading, the party turned to what she had identified as a locking bar: a mechanism, she said, that could be drawn back by force once the counterweight was freed. Two of the strongest among them set themselves against it. The iron did not move. They braced and heaved again, and a sound came from within the vault — not of mechanism yielding, but of something seating itself more firmly shut. She had not read a lock. She had read a seal, and their effort had not opened it but drawn the bolt home. The door was now shut in a way it had not been before they touched it.

They tried twice more before they understood what they had done. Then they went out through the reed-beds the way they had come, moving back into the risen light, the vault behind them more thoroughly closed than when they had arrived.

# Of the Expedition Beyond the Marsh Reaches

In the year the compact turned its eyes eastward, a party went out into the fens where the old causeway sinks beneath the water and the reed-cutters do not go. Their purpose was to reach the vault beyond — a place the elders knew of by report, sealed since the last generation had memory of it, holding what the compact required. They went prepared to argue their claim before any who would hear it, for the way through the marsh was held.

The wardens of that passage were three, stationed where the causeway stones first vanish into the brown water. They were not hostile men, but they were watchful, and their authority over that ground was old and unquestioned among the fen-dwellers. The party's speaker went forward alone and set out the compact's right of way — not by force of arms, not by bluster, but by reciting the compact that the compact's founders had struck with the fen-holds two generations prior, when the wardens' grandfathers had built that very gatehouse from stone hauled up from the causeway's ruins. The compact had never been formally dissolved; it had only been forgotten. The speaker had not forgotten it. The wardens deliberated until evening, and when the reed-light was lit in the gatehouse window they came out and stood aside. Therefore the party passed into the inner fen before dark.

The ferryman who worked the deeper channels was another matter. He was an old man who had heard compacts invoked before and held that compacts did not keep his hull caulked. He named his price for the crossing and the use of his pole in the reed-maze beyond, and the price was steep — not extortionate, but steep, the kind of fee that strips a purse down to its leather. The party paid it. They understood that argument would cost more time than coin, and time in the fens, where the water rises with the tide even this far from the sea, was the thing they had least of. The coins went into the ferryman's jar, the jar went under his seat, and he took up his pole without another word. Therefore they reached the far shore by midnight.

What waited at the vault's threshold was not a lock they could pick nor a guardian they could address. It was the vault itself: flooded to the knees in the outer chamber, cold as the water that fed it, and the mechanism that opened the inner door was corroded to a single stubborn lever that required a weight the ferryman had not warned them of. They worked at it in turns through what remained of the night — cold to the waist, hands slipping, the water rising another finger's width toward morning. Each time one of them flagged, another took the lever. They did not speak much. There was an understanding among them, settled in the long hours, that they would not go back empty, and that understanding held where argument or exhortation would have broken. When the lever finally turned, the grinding of stone on stone was the loudest sound the fen had made in years.

They came home across the causeway in the grey of the following morning, the ferryman's crossing behind them and the warden's gate opening without ceremony at their approach. The compact's business in the marsh reaches was done.

The compact sent three of its own into the fenlands at the close of a grey autumn, when the reed-beds had gone to rust and the water lay still beneath a sky the colour of old iron. Their charge was to reach the sunken wayhouse at the heart of the marsh, where records of the old agreements were said to rest beneath the flood — records the compact needed, and would not trust to any messenger.

They knew the marsh had a keeper: a warden-spirit of the old persuasion, bound to those waters by compacts no longer living in any mouth. It did not sleep, but it was patient, and they had decided that patience could be outwitted. They split their approach — one went ahead on higher ground to draw the warden's attention eastward, while the other two waded west through shallow channels, waist-deep in black water, moving soft. For a time it seemed the ruse of noise and motion would hold. Then the warden turned.

It did not turn for the decoy. It turned for the two in the water, and it came with the speed of current, and the reeds bent before it, and the two stood in the open with nowhere to go that was not already marsh. The third came down from the ridge, and they were all three held.

A warden-spirit of that age does not destroy trespassers — it presses them and asks the debt. The debt is old and the terms have not changed: the marsh gives nothing without receiving. They understood this, because one of them had read what she could before they set out, and had believed it might come to it.

She took from her pack a bundle of letters — the only correspondence that had passed between her and her master in the years before she joined the compact, kept because there was no replacing them. She pressed the bundle beneath the surface with both hands and held it there until the water took it. She did not look up.

The warden drew back. The weight on the other two eased. Before them, water pulled away from the wayhouse threshold — just enough, and just long enough. They crossed through, found what they had come for, and turned for home.
