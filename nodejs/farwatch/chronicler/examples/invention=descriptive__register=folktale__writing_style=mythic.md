# Of the Expedition to the Sunken Archive

Hear, then, what befell the company that descended into the drowned vault beneath the old causeway, in the season when the marsh-water runs dark with peat and the reeds stand high enough to swallow a rider.

They had learned of the place through the usual channels — a fragment traded for coin, a dying man's boast taken seriously by those with the patience to piece such things together. What lay below the causeway's cracked pilings was said to be old beyond the reckoning of the current age: a chamber sealed when the waters rose, left intact because nothing that remained above ground had the knowing to open it. The company went down to test that claim.

They went in the last hour before dawn, when the marsh-wardens who walked the causeway's length were thickest in their boredom and most narrow in their watching. The company did not go by the road. They went through the reeds on their bellies, one hand always trailing in the cold water, moving no faster than the slow drift of surface weed. They had chosen their path in daylight three days prior, counting the wardens' steps, learning the long pauses at the south pillar where the footing was uncertain. So they moved through the gaps, slipping into the black water beneath the causeway's foundations without a sound to mark their passage. The wardens never turned their heads. Therefore the company found themselves in the vault's antechamber, dripping and cold, with no alarm raised against them.

What met them in that antechamber was not entirely unexpected — something had been set to keep the dark, though whether it had been placed there by design in the old age or had merely settled into the space as creatures will when left undisturbed long enough, none could say with certainty. It came at them from the far wall, low and fast, and the company's first blade caught it across the shoulder and turned it; the second opened it fully. The fight was brief and ugly in the way that fights in confined spaces always are — no room for footwork, the ceiling pressing down, the water underfoot slicked with what had spilled — but the company held their ground and the thing did not. Therefore they pressed on, into the deeper dark beyond.

The inner door was the work of minds that had thought carefully about permanence. It was stone set in stone, seamless at first glance, but ringed about its frame with markings that had clearly been put there to serve a purpose — characters cut with precision, arranged in a sequence that ran from the lintel down both jambs and met again at the threshold. The company's most learned member brought a light close and read for a long while. He read the way a man does when he recognizes the shape of a language but cannot find the meaning inside it — a hand tracing the cuts, lips moving without words forming, the long silence of a mind working at something that will not open. He tried three approaches to the inscription: the formal grammar of the deep scholars, the older root-tongue that predates the current letters, and lastly a kind of comparative reading that set the characters against similar carvings he had seen in ruins further south. None of it yielded. The door gave back nothing but the echo of his breathing. But the door remained shut, and the silence of the chamber told them plainly that whatever knowledge the inscription required, theirs was not sufficient to supply it.

They might have turned back then. They did not, because the company had come too far and paid too much in cold and effort to go home with empty hands if any other recourse remained. So they tried the door as a simple obstacle of stone and will. Three of them set their shoulders to it in the recessed frame where the gap, though invisible to the eye, could be felt by pressing fingers. They heaved in long sustained efforts, and they wedged iron into the join and applied leverage, and one of them climbed the frame and braced both feet against the lintel to add a downward press while the others pushed. The door gave not the breadth of a finger. It had not been sealed by weight alone but by some principle of joining the company did not understand, and muscle spent itself against it without purchase. When the last man stepped back, his breath loud in the chamber, they understood at last that the door would remain a door, and that they would not pass it.

They came back up through the causeway water as the sky was beginning to pale. The wardens were changing their watch. The company slipped through the reeds again, by the same path, and climbed out into the grey morning on the far bank with nothing to show for the descent but their bruises and their wet clothes and the carved shapes still turning, unresolved, in the learned man's memory.

The vault's inner chamber waits still beneath the causeway, its door unread and unforced, keeping whatever it was set to keep. The company came back from the marsh as they entered it: together, and empty-handed.

# The Account of the Southward Reaching, as Set Down in the Third Generation

There are expeditions mounted in haste and expeditions mounted in wisdom, and the southern reaching — the one they would afterward call the long courtesy — was mounted in wisdom, which is perhaps why it is still spoken of at all.

The party had gone to recover something owed to the compact, a debt of place rather than coin, in a valley that had passed through three sets of hands in the years since the founding. The rightful road to that valley ran through the holdings of a lord who had come into his lands by marriage rather than bloodline — a careful man, proud of his tenure, and not at all inclined to grant passage to those who carried old claims through his new country. A lesser expedition would have found another road. But the compact's people read the shape of the land, and there was no other road worth the name, and therefore they walked through the gates of the holding and asked for a hearing.

What followed was a long work of the tongue. The party set themselves before the lord and his council not as supplicants and not as rivals, but as neighbours in a matter of mutual interest — and they named the interest plainly. The valley's debt ran to the compact, they said, but the compact's presence in that valley would be no threat to the holding's grazing rights on its western edge; they proposed instead an arrangement that preserved the lord's access and asked only safe conduct in return. They answered his suspicions without brushing them aside, and when his steward raised the old boundary quarrel, they had the words for that too. By the evening fire, the lord had given his hand on it. The gates were opened.

But the road beyond the holding passed through the old drover's market at the edge of the marsh, and the marsh-wardens did not traffic in courtesy. They trafficked in silver.

The compact's people understood this. They had brought silver. Not a bribe in the way of a bribe — no pressed coins and averted eyes — but an honest and generous payment for the wardens' knowledge of the safe crossing-paths, the ones that did not swallow a man to the knee or deeper when the spring seep was still running. And the wardens, being practical souls, accepted the weight of it, and gave a guide, and the guide took the party through the drowned places by the high route of cut reeds, threading the causeways that only a warden could read, and the crossing that had turned back three expeditions in the decade prior was behind them before the noon sun had turned.

So it was that they came, in good order and on good ground, to the valley itself.

The valley did not welcome them. Valleys do not welcome; they are simply there. But this one had a quality to it — a silence that was not merely quiet, a weight in the air that pressed against the chest and filled the mind with the particular kind of doubt that whispers _turn back, this is not yours, you have no business here._ Those who have walked long-abandoned places know the feeling. The compact's people knew it. They had spoken of it before the departure, had named it plainly among themselves as a thing they would encounter, and therefore when it settled over them in that place — cold as marsh-water, heavy as old stone — it settled over people who had already decided not to be moved by it.

They made their camp. They kept their watches. When the silence deepened in the fourth hour of the night and the oldest among them woke with the iron certainty that he had made a terrible error, he lay still in his bedroll and remembered that he had made no error, and presently the feeling passed. In the morning they rose and did what they had come to do, and they did it without ceremony or hurry, because hurry would have meant the doubt had taken root.

They came home in the late summer, and what they brought back was not a thing easily shown, but neither is it easily taken away. The valley was theirs again, in the way that mattering to the compact makes a thing ours — through the long courtesy, through the patient silver, through the night that did not break them.

# The Crossing at the Grey Hour

They came to the crossing in the hour before dawn — that flat, still time when the water holds no colour and the light has not yet decided what it will do. It had been told to them, by those who had made such passages before, that the thing which watched that threshold kept its attention elsewhere in those hours; that it drowsed, or turned its face toward some other quarter of the marsh, and that a party careful with their feet and sparing of their breathing might slip across the shallows unseen.

They believed this. They went in single file, keeping to the shallower water where the reed-beds might break their outline, and they went in silence.

But the thing did not drowse.

It came up from the deeper channel without warning, without so much as a ripple to mark its rising, and it moved between the party and the far bank with the unhurried certainty of a creature that had watched this crossing for longer than any of them had lived. The reeds gave the party nothing. The grey light gave them nothing. They stood exposed in the shallows, the near bank too far behind to regain before the thing could reach them, the far bank sealed. The ruse — the careful timing, the pressed silence, the patience they had spent all the night before gathering — had been read through as though it were glass.

They might have fought. What turned them against it was not lack of will but the swift and settled recognition, passed between them in the space of a shared glance, that the thing before them was not an enemy to be overcome. It was a condition to be met. Fighting it would only spend them and leave the crossing still shut.

It was the eldest of the party who saw what the condition was. She had been in such places before, had read the older accounts of thresholds that do not answer to cunning or to strength but only to price; and she waded forward not in challenge but in the manner of one coming to settle what is owed. She drew the knife she wore at her left hip — the one she had never used for any quarrel, only for the rites her grandmother had taught her — and she opened her palm above the dark water. What she said to the guardian as she did so, she did not repeat to the others, and they did not ask. There are forms of offering that diminish in the telling.

The blood fell into the water. The guardian read what had been given and found it sufficient. It did not step aside so much as it ceased to be present in the way of obstacle — the way a locked gate ceases to be a gate when the right key turns it — and the crossing lay open.

They crossed. All of them crossed. The shallows bore their feet without complaint, the reeds parted, and the far bank came up to meet them as the first grey light began at last to decide it would become morning. What they had come into that marsh to find, they found on the other side; and when they turned for home, they carried it with them.

The eldest bound her palm with a strip of cloth and did not speak of the cost. Those who come after her know only that the price was paid, and that it was enough.
