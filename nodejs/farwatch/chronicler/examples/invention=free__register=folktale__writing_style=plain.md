# The Ashenmere Account

It is told of Tessaly Croft and the company she kept — Bram Oller and the twins called Neth and Nersa, and old Corwick who carried the maps — that they went into the Ashenmere Barrows in the autumn of the grey year, and they came back without what they had gone in for. This is the account of that going, set down as the company reported it, and as Corwick's maps confirm.

They entered before first light, when the barrow-wardens had pulled their cloaks tight and their eyes had gone heavy with the cold. Tessaly had watched the patrols for two nights running, had counted the gaps, and so she knew exactly where the watch grew thin: the northern approach, where the ground dips into the old drainage channel and the two guard-posts cannot see each other. The company went through that gap one at a time, low and slow, Corwick last with his satchel held against his chest to keep the tubes from knocking. They were inside the outer passage before the sky paled. That much went their way.

Deeper in, the passage opened into the vaulted antechamber, and there they found that the barrows were not so empty as the maps had promised. A body of hired blades had made camp among the sarcophagi — eight of them, armed, though not expecting company. Tessaly did not hesitate. She had the advantage of position and surprise, and she used both. Neth took the nearest man before he could call out; Bram drove the group away from the passage mouth so they could not retreat into the dark; and Tessaly herself held the center, pressing them into a corner from which there was no organizing a defense. The fight was short and total. The hired blades broke and fled the way they had come in, and the company let them go. There was work still to do, and no time to spend chasing men who were already beaten.

So far, therefore, it had gone well. They pressed on into the inner passage, toward the sealed chamber the maps named as the Repository, and this is where the trouble began.

The door was covered in inscription — carved rings of script running inward from the frame to the lock-face, script in the old temple hand that Corwick had spent thirty years studying. He set his lamp close and read. He read for a long time. Then he read again, because the first reading had not given him a sequence that made sense, and he thought he had misread the verb-roots. The company waited, and the cold came up through the stone, and Neth wrapped her hands in her cloak. At last Corwick spoke the words aloud, in the order he believed the inscription demanded — the invocation, the key-phrase, the release — and touched his fingers to the lock-face in the prescribed places. Nothing moved. The rings of inscription did not shift. The door stood.

Corwick tried again with a different reading. He had been certain, the first time; now he was less certain. His second attempt was for a sequence he trusted less, a translation he had always found doubtful in the old liturgical texts, and it did not work either. The door stood. He stepped back. He did not try a third time, because by then he understood that the fault was not in the execution but in the understanding — that there was something in that inscription he did not know, some layer of the old temple hand that his training had not reached, and that no amount of re-reading would supply what was simply not there. He told Tessaly this plainly. She believed him.

What she would not do was leave without one more attempt. The door was old — older than the blades who had camped in the antechamber, older than the barrows themselves, perhaps, set in here when the passage was built — and old things can be broken even when they cannot be opened. Bram Oller was the strongest among them, and Neth and Nersa were not small. The three of them set their shoulders to it together, Corwick watching the lock-face for any sign of movement, and they pushed. They braced and pushed again. Tessaly added her weight. The door was fitted into the stone so precisely that there was no gap for a lever, and without a gap there was nothing to work against. They could feel the weight of it, the impossible settled weight, the way it was simply part of the mountain. It did not give. It did not creak. It did not move at all.

They left it standing.

Corwick has since written that the inscription is of a dialect he has encountered in one other place, a fragment from the eastern reaches, and that he believes a complete study might yield the reading he lacked. Whether anyone will go back to use that knowledge is a question the chronicle does not answer. What the chronicle records is this: that the company of Tessaly Croft went into the Ashenmere Barrows, went in cleanly and fought well, and came to the door of the Repository, and the door would not open to them, and they came home.

# The Crossing to Vael Dort

_As set down in the forty-third year of the Compact's keeping._

---

In the autumn of that year, when the passes had not yet hardened with frost but the lowland roads ran thick with mud, the Compact sent four of its people east toward the walled town of Vael Dort, to recover what had been promised and left too long unclaimed.

The leader of that party was Edrea Sorn, who had kept the eastern roads before and knew their habits. With her went the brothers Cael and Pitt, who were handy enough in a dispute, and old Mirren, who had once lived in Dort and remembered its gates.

The first trouble met them before they reached the river crossing at Thallow Ford — not a battle, but a blockade. The Herath Company, a band of armed collectors loyal to no writ but their own, had spread themselves across the road and named a toll that was more theft than tariff. Edrea did not reach for iron. She rode forward alone and spent an hour in conversation with the Company's captain, a red-bearded man named Osker who fancied himself a lord of the road. She spoke to him of the Compact's long memory — not as a threat, but as a statement of fact — and of the merchants whose custom Osker lived on, and of how word traveled between market towns about which roads were safe and which were grief. She reminded him, gently, that the Compact was known to keep ledgers. Osker listened. By the end of it he waved them through without a coin changing hands, and even had one of his men point out the best ford.

So they crossed the river and came into the eastern lowlands, where the fields had been harvested bare and the villages sat quiet and watchful. Here the second difficulty was not one they could argue their way past. The road into Vael Dort ran through the holding of a man called Aldric Fenne, who had shut his gate across it — a legal gate, built on his own land, with his own right to levy passage. The fee he named was steep, meant to be argued down, but Edrea did not argue. The Compact had been given resources for the journey and she had kept them carefully. She paid Aldric Fenne his price, in good silver, counted out on the lid of a traveling chest in full view of his servants. Fenne had been prepared for a quarrel and the straightforward payment seemed to unsettle him; he opened his gate with nothing more than a nod, and they went on.

They arrived at Vael Dort in the grey of an afternoon and were lodged in the Compact's old house there, which a caretaker had kept against their coming. What they had come to retrieve was held in a dispute — a claim on it had been raised by one of the town's guild councils since last the Compact had sent anyone to press the matter, and the guild was in no hurry to resolve it. The council met slowly and deliberated at length, and when Edrea brought the party's documentation before the sitting magistrate, he received it politely and named a date for a hearing two weeks hence.

Two weeks they waited in Vael Dort, and this was the hardest passage of the journey — not for any danger, but for the weight of days going nowhere. The guild sent word twice, through intermediaries, suggesting that perhaps an arrangement might be made, hinting at coin or concession. Edrea refused both approaches without ceremony. Cael and Pitt, who were men of action and found idleness poorly suited to them, grew short-tempered by the end of the first week; she kept them to their tasks and did not let them quarrel with anyone of consequence. On the appointed day, the party appeared before the magistrate as required, with every document in order and their account of the Compact's claim given plainly and without embellishment. The guild, who had perhaps expected them to give up or negotiate away some part of what they were owed, found instead that the party had simply waited and returned and asked again for what was theirs. The magistrate ruled in the Compact's favor that same afternoon.

They came home before the passes closed, ahead of the first real snow, having settled what had been unsettled and returned with nothing less than they had gone to claim.

There came a year when the compact sent four into the Ashwood — or so the old names had it, though by the time of this expedition the wood had swallowed three generations of waymarkers and the paths through it were more rumor than road.

They went in at first light, which was prudent, and they moved carefully, which was also prudent. But the Ashwood keeps a warden, and always has — a thing old enough that it remembers the trees before they were trees. It does not sleep, and it does not mistake. When the four tried to pass the deep crossing by moving low and quiet along the eastern bank, stepping where the moss swallows sound and keeping to the shadow of the overhang, they were found anyway. The warden heard what their feet did not make: the small displacement of air, the warmth of them against the cold stone. It rose from the water, and it looked at them, and there was no pretending they had not come.

Here is where many expeditions have ended.

The warden at the deep crossing is not a thing you fight. It is a thing you satisfy.

The one they call Hesta — the accounts give her a wool-dyer's hands and a merchant's eye, which is all we know of her now — understood this before the others did. She had carried something into the Ashwood that was not required by the mission. A sealed case of some kind; the testimonies do not say what was inside. She said nothing to her companions. She walked to the water's edge where the warden waited, and she set the case down, and she opened it, and she stepped back.

The warden took what was inside. It took a long time about it. The accounts say the four stood in the cold for the better part of an hour, watching something they chose not to describe when they came home. Then the warden sank, and the crossing was clear, and they walked over it and went on.

They found what they had come for. They came back through the wood and the wood let them leave.

What was in the case, Hesta never said, and no one pressed her. In a compact that has sent parties into places like the Ashwood for as long as the records go back, you learn that a warden satisfied is a warden satisfied, and the price of that is between the one who paid it and whatever received it.
