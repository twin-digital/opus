# Of the Company That Sought the Ossuary of Karath Venn

The company of seven left Thornholt in the grey hours before dawn, when the frost still held the grass flat and the only sound was the iron creak of their boots on the old stone road. Their charge was a thing that required quiet hands and strong ones both: to enter the Ossuary of Karath Venn — that sealed place in the chalk hills where the old compact had been laid down in rites that no living practitioner now understood — and to recover from its innermost chamber whatever form the covenant's founding instrument had taken.

They came to the outer works at midday, when mist still clung in the hollow and the sentinels kept to their fires. Mira of the Long Step — she who could walk dry leaf without sound — took the lead, and the company followed in her footsteps as she had drilled them: weight on the outside edge, the next foot placed before the last was lifted, breath kept to a slow and measured interval. The sentinels were three, posted at the broken arch that served as the ossuary's only visible approach. The company went wide through the elder scrub, moving in a line so tight that each member could feel the warmth of the one ahead, and came to the chalk face on the far side of the rise without the sentinels' fire ever changing in its quality of burning. They descended through a crack in the hillside that old Fenwick had marked in the survey maps — narrow enough to scrape both shoulders, steep enough to require the hands, but true — and so they came inside.

The inner halls were lit by the greenish bioluminescence of the chalk itself, that slow cold glow that the old builders had cultivated by some art now lost, and it showed them the guardians before the guardians saw them. These were not sentinels set to watch but something older — wardens who had perhaps stood their post since the ossuary's sealing, four of them, armoured in plate gone dark with age and draped in the heraldry of a house three centuries dead. They were roused by Fenwick's boot catching a fallen corbel stone; the sound rang off the chalk walls and the wardens turned as one, and what followed was not long. Aldric and Brant took the first two before they had weapons drawn; the third fought and fought hard, and put Brant down with a blow to the shoulder that would trouble him the rest of the expedition, but Mira came around its flank when it over-extended and ended the matter there; the fourth held back, then fled deeper into the ossuary, and Senna ran it down in the corridor beyond. The company pressed on.

The inner chamber was at the end of a long processional hall — a round room, low-ceilinged, its walls carved from floor to crown in a script that none of them had ever encountered: not the old compact tongue, not the liturgical register, not the merchant short-hand that Fenwick could sight-read. Something older. Something that had perhaps been already old when the builders dressed the chalk. Old Fenwick stood before it for the better part of an hour, his candle moving slow along the carved lines, his lips shaping sounds that led nowhere. He had spent forty years in the covenant's libraries; he had read things most living scholars had only heard attributed; and he came away from that wall carrying nothing. The symbols did not merely resist him the way an unfamiliar script resists — they seemed to have been drained of meaning deliberately, the forms of language preserved while the substance was excised, so that the eye moved along the lines and arrived at silence. Whatever key was needed to unlock the sanctum beyond, it was not knowledge that any of them carried.

Therefore they tried force.

The sanctum entrance was a single dressed slab of chalk-stone, set into the wall without visible seam or hinge. Aldric put his shoulder to it first; the chalk took the blow without complaint and his shoulder reported the contact in terms he did not enjoy. They worked the picks they had brought for the possibility of digging, angling the heads into the invisible joint at the slab's perimeter — and the chalk resisted, not as ordinary chalk resists, which is to say not at all, but as something that wore chalk's face and had stone's intransigence at its heart. Brant, his wounded shoulder bound and useless, directed the effort with his good arm from where he sat, but directing is not the same as doing, and the four whole members of the company threw themselves at that slab in every combination of angle and implement they could devise over two exhausting hours. The slab did not open. It did not crack. It did not yield a hair's breadth, and left their hands bloodied and their breath spent and the chamber precisely as they had found it.

They came home without the instrument. They came home through the same crack in the chalk face, the same elder scrub, the same frost-hardened road, and they carried with them Brant's wrapped shoulder and Aldric's reddened hands and the knowledge, heavy as the chalk that had refused them, that the Ossuary of Karath Venn had given up nothing. Whatever lay sealed in that innermost chamber would remain sealed until someone came who could read what Fenwick could not read, or open what no force of arm had opened.

That someone was not this company, on this expedition. The covenant's founding instrument, if it lay there still, lay there yet.

# The Vetharn Accord: Being an Account of the Emissary-Journey of Sybille Auch and Her Companions, in the Thirty-First Year of the Covenant

The party that rode out under Sybille Auch numbered four, and they carried no weapons worth naming — only sealed letters, a coffer of coin, and the Covenant's authority, which is to say they carried everything and nothing, depending on whom they met at the other end of the road.

The matter was this: the Warden of Vetharn, one Cressin Dault, had closed the river crossing at Grayford to all covenant traffic, ostensibly over a dispute in tolls that his father had begun and that he had inherited without particular feeling either way. But Grayford was the only fording within three days' ride that did not require passing through Aldenmere, and Aldenmere had its own complications, and so the Covenant needed Cressin Dault to open his ford before the autumn rains made the question moot for another season. Sybille understood this. She also understood that Cressin Dault, from what the Covenant's factors reported, was a man who had spent his youth watching his father conduct himself with great noise and small result, and had determined to be nothing like him.

They arrived at Vetharn on a morning of low cloud, the kind that flattens a landscape and makes everything look like something else. The hall was built for a grander household than currently occupied it — long tables laid for forty, fewer than a dozen present when Sybille and her companions were shown inside. Cressin Dault sat at the far end, younger than she'd expected, with the particular stillness of a man who had recently decided to stop performing and was watching to see whether the world noticed.

He did not offer them seats.

Sybille had met this before — the silence deployed as an opening position. She did not rush to fill it. When she spoke, she did not speak of the ford, or the tolls, or the Covenant's needs. She spoke of the last survey of the eastern drainages, which the Covenant's cartographers had completed the previous spring — work that concerned Vetharn's western boundary in ways that Cressin's father had never bothered to resolve, and which left certain grazing rights in a condition of useful ambiguity. She had the relevant abstracts in her satchel. She did not produce them immediately. She described what they contained, and then she described what she understood the Warden's interest in the matter to be, and she was careful to be accurate, because she had done the work of learning his interest before she came.

She was not offering him a bargain. She was demonstrating that she had taken him seriously enough to understand what he actually wanted, which no one, she suspected, had bothered to do in some time. The distinction mattered to him. She watched it matter.

By the end of that hour, Cressin Dault had offered them seats, and the conversation had shifted into something that more nearly resembled a negotiation between parties who intended to reach an agreement.

---

The terms, when they arrived at them, had a shape that Sybille had more or less anticipated: the Covenant would acknowledge the Vetharn grazing rights, in writing, and Grayford would reopen. But there was the matter of the tolls in arrears, which Cressin's father had accumulated as a grievance and which Cressin had no strong feeling about but could not simply set aside without appearing to. Fifteen months of back-toll, at the old rate, before the new arrangement could take effect.

Sybille sent one of her companions — a steady man named Orveth who had spent twenty years handling the Covenant's material accounts — back to the coffer they had brought. The sum was not comfortable to part with, but it had been placed there for exactly this purpose, and Orveth counted it out on the table with the unhurried competence of a man who found the handling of money neither exciting nor shameful. Coin speaks most clearly when it is not held theatrically, and Cressin Dault watched the counting with an expression that, by its end, had resolved into something like relief. The arrears were a knot he had not known how to cut. The Covenant cut it for him, and charged the cost of the cutting to the value of an open road.

---

That evening, with the ledger terms settled and the draft agreement drawn, Cressin's steward raised a complication.

The Warden, he said, wished to include a clause requiring the Covenant's neutrality in any dispute between Vetharn and the township of Cellin, whose charter the Covenant had historically been seen to favor. Sybille read the proposed addition twice. Then she set it down and asked for wine, and while the wine was being brought she looked at the fire and thought about what was being asked.

It was not a small thing. Cellin sat at the confluence of three trade roads. The Covenant had obligations there, not merely habits. To sign away its voice in Cellin's affairs for the sake of a river crossing was to trade something real for something useful, and the arithmetic did not work, and she knew it, and more to the point the Covenant knew it — had known it might come to this, and had told her before she left that if it did, she was to hold.

She held.

Not through argument — she had already made the argument, in the afternoon session, and it had not moved the steward, who was not genuinely persuaded and was not trying to be. She held through the long, uncomfortable evening that followed, through a supper that was served and eaten without the matter being resolved, through a night in a guest room where the shutters let in a draft she did not bother to block because the cold sharpened her attention. In the morning she was at the table before Cressin Dault's household had finished its bread.

She had, by that point, held the same position for sixteen hours. She did not show the seams of it. She was not performing patience — she had made the calculation clearly enough that impatience was not available as an option. The clause would break the agreement, and she was not permitted to let it break on their side.

Cressin Dault came to breakfast without his steward. He sat down across from her, looked at her with the same particular stillness she had noted on arrival, and withdrew the clause without explanation. She did not express relief. She thanked him, and they signed, and the copies were sealed, and by midmorning Sybille Auch and her companions were on the road south with Grayford opened behind them and the Vetharn accord folded inside her satchel, where it would ride back to the Covenant's hall and be entered into the record as a thing accomplished — quiet, and costly, and permanent.

The Warden of Vetharn stood in his doorway and watched them go. What he made of the business was his own affair. The chronicle records only that he watched until they were past the ridge, and then went inside.

# Of the Passage Through the Veilfen

They came to the Veilfen at the last hour of darkness, when the mist lay thickest and a figure three steps ahead was little more than a shape against the grey. Kael had read the night and given his counsel plainly: move through the reed-beds before the light came, before whatever held this ground could turn its attention toward the path. The others had agreed without much spoken. They stripped their gear of anything that might catch or clatter, wound cloth around the shaft of every lantern, left the lanterns cold. The fen demanded silence; they offered it.

But the fen had its own counsel.

Where Maret stepped — carefully, heel-to-toe, testing the soft ground before trusting it — the earth gave way without warning, and she went in past the knee with a sound like wet cloth torn across a stone. The noise carried. Over still water it always does, finding distance before a body can move to reclaim it. What came out of the reeds was not loud. It was simply there — close, sudden, and present in the way old things are present: not arrived, but revealed. The attention of the place fell on them from every side at once. The path forward had become a wall.

They could not contest what held that ground — the water made flight a slow and noisy thing — and Kael knew it. What surrounded them was patient in the manner of things that have outlasted every covenant this land had known and expected to outlast those yet to come. It was owed something. It would not move until it received it.

So Tessyn drew the good knife — the one his father had put in his hand the morning he left the valley and had not asked him to return — and cut his palm open over the dark water. He did it without theatre, without a long pause for resolve. He simply cut, and held the wound wide, and let the blood fall. It fell in drops at first, then in a thin, steady thread, and the water took it down without a ripple, the way stone takes ink. He said the words his grandmother had pressed into him, the old words he had repeated as a child until they were emptied of meaning and became only rhythm and breath, which is, he had come to understand, precisely what they were for: the shape of a giving that asks nothing back.

The watching withdrew. Not all at once — it retreated as cold retreats from a room when a fire catches, by degrees that a body registers before the mind names them. The pressure eased from their shoulders. The reeds settled. The mist, which had felt thick with intent, became ordinary mist again, vapour over water, nothing more. The path lay open.

They went on into the last of the dark, and the Veilfen let them through.
