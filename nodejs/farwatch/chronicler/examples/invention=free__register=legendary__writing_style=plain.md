# The Reckoning of the Var Osk Expedition

In the forty-third year of the Compact, Maren Ashveil led three of the covenant's sworn — Davan Cord, Silt, and old Brece — into the broken country east of the Threnwall, where the stronghold of Var Osk still stood above the reed flats, abandoned since the Undoing of the Hollow Lords but never, as the covenant had reason to believe, emptied.

They went by night. Cord had learned the movements of the Pale Remnant, the mindless wardens that yet walked Var Osk's outer court, and so the party crossed the rubble-choked approach in silence, moving when the wardens moved away, going still when they turned back. Brece lost a sandal in the mire and tied a strip of his cloak around his foot and kept going. They came over the outer wall before the eastern watch turned and dropped into the inner court without a sound raised.

Therefore they reached the keep's ground floor intact — but a Remnant warden had been posted within, not without, and it found them in the low hall before they had gone twenty paces. It was a large thing, armored in the fused plate of the old lords, and it swung a halberd without speech or warning. Ashveil and Cord drew it between them while Silt drove a spike through the gap at its gorget and Brece took its halberd arm with a short axe. It went down after a long struggle that left Cord limping and the hall slicked with a dark fluid that was not blood. They bound Cord's knee and pressed on, because the seal room lay one floor below and they had not come this far to stop.

The seal room was as the covenant's old records had described it: a circular chamber, its floor carved with the interlocking script of the Hollow Lords, its far wall dominated by a door of black iron set with a mechanism of brass rings and sliding tiles. No lock that a key could open — a cipher, meant to be solved. Ashveil had studied the Hollow Lords' notation for two winters in the covenant's library. She knelt before the mechanism and read the tiles by lamplight, working the brass rings through the positions the old texts prescribed.

The door did not open. She tried the secondary interpretation, the one Brece had argued for during those long winters of study. The mechanism moved, but the door held. She tried again, slower, tracing each tile in sequence. The rings slid and caught and would not complete their course. Something in the records was wrong, or something in the room had changed, or the notation admitted a meaning none of them had reached — and they had no more interpretations to try.

Therefore Davan Cord, leg bound and temper short, set his shoulder against the iron door. Silt joined him, then Brece, all three pressing while Ashveil worked the mechanism from the side. The door did not shift. They dragged a broken column from the outer hall and used it as a ram. The brass mechanism cracked at one ring and the tiles jammed and would not move at all after that. The door had not opened so much as a finger's width, and now the mechanism was spoiled.

They stood in the lamp-lit chamber with nothing to show for it, and Cord's leg was swelling badly, and the way out was still guarded by whatever else walked Var Osk's halls. Ashveil ordered them out. She marked the floor's script in charcoal on a square of vellum and carried it away with her, because there was nothing else to carry.

They came home in four days, cold and without what they had gone to find.

**Of the Drovek Passage, and Its Opening**

In the third autumn of the Harrow Years, the covenant sent Maret Voss and four companions south to treat with Lord Aldric Drovek at his seat above the Narrows. The Narrows was the one road between the covenant's quarter and the valley markets, and Drovek had shut it to all traffic but his own traders since his eldest son was taken in the Bristone raid two seasons prior — a raid the covenant had no hand in, but whose memory had soured all dealings between them. Letters had not moved him. Maret went in person, unarmed and flying no banner, because she understood that a man still in his grief could only be reached by a face that would sit across from his own and be accountable to it.

Drovek received them in a hall stripped of its hangings. He was not a subtle man in his mourning, and Maret did not try to meet him subtly. She set aside the question of the raid and put before him instead the question of his gates. She had learned, before setting out, which merchants had begun routing three days east to avoid the shut pass, and she named them. She named the tolls they no longer paid. She named the buyers in the valley who had found other factors, and whose loyalty to Drovek's traders was, season by season, going thin. She did not lecture him; she let him see the arithmetic of his own position. He did not concede that evening, but he asked what the covenant proposed, and asking was itself a turning.

His price, named in the morning, was steep: two years of reduced toll collected in advance, and a surety in coin besides, to be held against any future grievance. The sum was more than the council had authorized. Maret's companions said nothing, but she felt their hesitation as clearly as if they had spoken. She weighed the road against the coin and judged that the council, shown an open pass, would ratify what she had agreed to; she could not bring them an open pass without first paying for it. She drew from the expedition's reserve, signed the covenant's mark to the deed, and accepted the terms.

The deed was done, but Drovek would not ride ceremony. He sent his own courier to the toll-house at the far end of the Narrows, and until that courier returned with confirmation, the pass was formally shut and the party was formally guests — guests who did not leave. They lodged in the hall's lower rooms and waited out the days. On the second night, one of Drovek's stewards sought Maret out and suggested, in the manner of men testing floors before they walk on them, that the lord had found himself wondering whether the advance might be revised. Maret looked at the steward for a moment and sent him back with a plain answer: the deed was signed, and she would wait for the courier. She said nothing more on the matter, and she did not revisit it when the steward found occasion to pass by her the following morning.

The courier came on the fourth day. The toll-house had the deed. The Narrows was open. Maret and her companions rode through it before the light failed, and the road beneath them — overgrown at the verges after two seasons of disuse, but sound — carried them north without incident.

She laid the deed and the toll-house's confirmation before the council on her return. The council ratified the terms she had made, and the road remained open through the seasons that followed.

**Of the Expedition to the Vault of Sevan Dross**

In the autumn of the covenant's thirty-second year, a party of three set out for the Vault of Sevan Dross, where the old compact had lain sealed since before any living member could say. Maren Ashcoat led them, with Toven the Younger at her right hand and the one called Reth Cord behind.

The Vault lay beneath the hill called the Quiet Shoulder, and the road to it passed through the grove of Oshin where the wardens still walked their circuits after dark — hollow things bound to an ancient charge, aware of heat and breath and the sound of boots on dry leaves. Maren had been told the wardens kept to the eastern margin in the hour before dawn, and that a careful party might cross the western reach without disturbing them. She believed it, and so they came to the grove in darkness, moving low through the undergrowth, their cloaks drawn against the cold.

But the intelligence was wrong, or the wardens had changed their habit. They had not reached the midpoint of the grove when the nearest warden turned from its circuit and stood among them. The sound it made was not a challenge but an alarm. By the time the three had cleared the grove's edge, the whole margin was awake and loud behind them, and the path they had meant to take lay blocked.

They came to the Vault's threshold from the wrong direction, exposed and known, and the door — a slab of pale stone fitted so closely it showed no seam — would not answer the old passwords. The compact that held the Vault was older than the passwords. Something else was owed.

Toven understood it first. He had carried a thing with him since boyhood: a sealed case of horn that held the name his mother had given him before the covenant renamed him. He had meant to open it in his own time, to read what he had been before his oath. He set the case on the threshold stone and stepped back, leaving it unclaimed, given to nothing and no one — which is to say, given to the compact itself.

The door opened inward. The stone moved without sound, as though it had been waiting for exactly that weight to settle on the threshold: not a treasure but a relinquishment.

They went in. They came back out.

Maren wrote in her own account, afterward, that she did not know whether what Toven gave was the thing the compact required or merely the thing that happened to satisfy it. The compact did not explain itself. The door still stood open when they left, as though it had no further business with them.
