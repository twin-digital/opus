# The Valdris Ossuary: An Account of the Company's Expedition in the Month of Ashening

The company moved on the Valdris Ossuary at the grey hour before first light — when the garrison's watch changes and eyes are heaviest. Maret had scouted the outer wall on three separate nights and marked a length of crumbled parapet on the eastern face where the guards' torchlight did not reach. They went over it one at a time, without a stone displaced or a voice raised, and were inside the precinct before the sky had lightened enough to show their shapes against it.

The inner court, however, held a different kind of keeper.

Two of the ossuary's bound wardens stood at the arch of the reliquary passage — tall things that had been men, once, and now moved with the jerked deliberateness of old rope pulled by unseen hands. There was no slipping past them; they were set to feel, not to see, and Maret had warned the company of this. Kael went in first with a short blade and drew the nearer warden hard to the left. Duna came in at its flank with the hammer while the second warden was still turning to face the threat. It cost Kael a bruise across the ribs where the thing caught him on the backswing, but both wardens were on the ground within the minute, and the passage stood open.

The reliquary chamber beyond was smaller than the records had promised. Every wall was carved from floor to vault with columns of Vethrani script — the inscription-tongue of the ossuary-builders, close-packed and unsectioned. Somewhere in those columns lay the spoken formula that would put a question to the inner sanctum's door, a question the door was bound by its own making to answer. Lirel had studied Vethrani for two years at the collegium and she set to work with her lamp held close to the stone, running her fingers down the columns, lips moving.

She worked for the better part of an hour. The script was neither corrupted nor erased; it was dense with layers of gloss and counter-gloss belonging to a tradition she had only the edges of. The formula she finally assembled and spoke aloud met the door with silence. She tried three variants, parsing back through the columns each time, looking for the error. The door gave nothing. What she lacked was not patience or care but the formation of a full Vethrani initiate, and that was not a thing to be improvised in a lamp-lit room under a dead man's roof.

The door remained, therefore, to be taken on other terms.

It was cut from a single slab of black ashlar fitted into its frame with a precision that left no gap for a lever and no crack for a wedge. The company had brought a wrecking bar of good cold iron on the chance that knowledge might fail them, and Kael and Brenn set their combined weight to it for a long time. The bar bent at the collar. The door did not shift by the width of a finger.

They went back through the reliquary passage, stepping around the warden-pieces, and out through the gap in the eastern wall as the grey light was broadening into something that would shortly make moving figures visible across the whole of the precinct. They came away with no more than bruised ribs and a bent bar, and the Valdris Ossuary closed behind them as it had stood before them: intact, and unread.

# From the Accounts of the Thorngate Compact: The Velchan Commission, Autumn of the Eleventh Year

The compact sent the company south in the third week of autumn, when the roads were still dry and the passes open. Their charge was to secure right of transit through the Velchan demesne — territory closed to compact couriers for eleven years, since the quarrel over the Ardenmere tolls had turned the lord's house cold.

They came to Velch Crossing on the fifth day. Maret, who had dealt with Velchan factors at the Keswick fair and knew they set store by lineage and protocol, spoke first to the border wardens. She did not present the company as compact agents — that word had poisoned too many conversations — but as independent merchants representing a consortium with interests in the south, and she carried letters from three houses the Velchans respected. The head warden, a careful man named Osso, listened without warmth but listened nonetheless. By midday the company was inside the gatehouse, waiting on the steward.

The steward's price was not passage. He wanted the Ardenmere debt settled — not in coin, but in kind: twelve bolts of Thorngate-grade dye-cloth, which the Velchans had been unable to source since the quarrel broke trade. The company had brought six bolts and what remained of the road-fund. Maret sent Coll back to the gates with the cloth and a purse heavy enough that Osso would not open it in front of the steward, and the steward signed the transit warrant before supper.

What followed was harder. The warrant allowed them through the Velchan lands, but the lord's cousin — a man named Pereth, who fancied himself the real authority on that road — intercepted them at the river ford and held the company at the waystation in Aldric's Bend for three days. He was not looking for coin. He was looking for impatience, for some small discourtesy that would give him cause to send them back. The company gave him none. They ate what he provided and thanked him for the shelter. On the second night Pereth sent someone to offer Maret wine and conversation; she accepted the wine and spoke pleasantly of nothing that mattered. On the third morning he came himself to the breakfast table and, finding nothing to object to, released them with a letter of safe conduct as far as the southern bridge.

They crossed it before noon.

# From the Chronicle of the Compact of the Threefold Gate: The Greyvast Crossing, Third Year

The Mirefen causeway was their one clean option, and they took it at the grey hour before dawn — single file along the planking, the fog sitting thick enough to muffle a cough at ten paces. The wardens of Greyvast are poor-sighted things; in fog, Aldric Pen reckoned, they would be little better than blind. The party moved quietly and kept their torches cold.

But the warden that found them that morning did not use its eyes. It came upwind along the causeway and was already between them and the far bank before anyone heard its step. When it gave its challenge — a low sound, more felt in the chest than heard — the fog had closed behind the party as thickly as before. The water on either side was black and still. There was nowhere to go.

They could not fight it, not there, not like that, and Aldric knew it. What he did instead was unwrap the Threefold Lamp from its oilskin — the compact's oldest piece, carried since the founding — and hold it out over the water with both hands. Not as a gift. As a reckoning. The wardens of Greyvast hold toll over any threshold crossed unbidden, and the Lamp was old enough, and sacred enough, to stand as payment for the trespass.

The warden looked at it for a long moment. Then it took the Lamp, stepped to the edge of the causeway, and let them pass.

The party crossed and completed what they had set out to do. They came home in better shape than the morning had promised. The Lamp stayed behind.
