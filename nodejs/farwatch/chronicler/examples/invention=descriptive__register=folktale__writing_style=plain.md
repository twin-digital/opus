There is a vault beneath the old causeway — those who know its name, if it has one, do not say it in easy company — and it was to that place the company turned their steps when word came that something kept below it might serve the compact's need. The marshes there have their own wardens, men who walk the fog-wrapped paths between the standing stones at the causey's end, and the company knew they could not pass those wardens openly, not with the compact's interest over them. So they went without it.

They moved through the reeds in the hour before the moon came up, when the marsh-wardens' torches were the only points of light. One by one they slipped between the pools, reading the ground for sound, choosing wet over dry when dry would creak, until they had brought themselves through the warden's ring and down to the stone stairs that descend below the causey. The wardens never turned. That much went well.

Below, the vault had a guardian of its own — not a man but something that had been shut in with the old things and had made itself at home among them. The company did not try to reason with it or go around it; they had seen its kind before and knew it would have followed them anyway. So they put it down in the close dark of the low passage, and though it cost them bruises and breath, they held the passage at the end of it. Therefore they came through to the inner chamber, the thing behind them and the vault before them.

Here is where the expedition broke.

The inner chamber held a lock that was not a lock — not a bolt to be drawn or a bar to be lifted, but a mechanism set into the door's face in the old manner, a series of marks and counter-marks that had to be turned in the right order before the door would consent to open. There was writing above it. The company had brought with them a man who knew old script, and he stood a long time with a torch raised to those marks. But the writing was older than his knowing — the dialect had shifted in the generations since the vault was sealed, or the stonecutter had worked in a hand no living scholar had learned to read; whichever it was, the marks gave him nothing, and the door kept its counsel.

They might have turned back then. They did not, because the need was real and the door was close, and people who have come so far through a marsh in the dark do not easily turn from a door that is only a door. So they tried what you try when knowing fails: one of them set his shoulder to it, and then two, and then the largest of them brought a pry-bar to the seam at the door's edge. The stone did not care. It had been made to hold against force as well as against ignorance, and the company had not the tools or the leverage to change its mind. When the pry-bar bent, they understood they were done.

They came back up through the passage, past the thing they had put down, and out through the reeds in the grey of early morning — the wardens still walking their circles, still not knowing who had come and gone. The vault remained shut. Whatever the compact had sent them to find stayed where it was, below the causeway, behind the door that would not yield, waiting for someone who could read what was written above it.

The company did not pretend otherwise when they gave their account. They had gone as far as the lock, and no further.

The company came to the marsh-wardens first, as any with sense would. The wardens held the only dry passage into the lowlands, and they were not a welcoming kind — hard people who kept their own counsel and their gates shut to strangers, and had reason enough for both. A lesser company might have wasted breath on demands, or worn impatience on their faces until the wardens closed ranks. Instead the company sent their speaker to the warden's hall without weapons drawn and without hurry, and that speaker did something the wardens had not expected: she listened.

She listened to the wardens' grievances, and they had many, in those years — the roads claimed by too many lords and the market towns grown stingy with their custom. She did not offer solutions they had not asked for. She named their difficulty back to them, plain and without flattery, and proposed only this: that her company be counted, for the span of their crossing, as the wardens' own escort. Their passage a service rendered, not a passage extracted. The wardens considered this a while, because they were not fools; but neither were they immune to being treated fairly. They opened the gate.

The old causeway beyond it was a different problem.

It had been built over drowned ground, and parts of it had gone back to the water in the years since any mason had tended it. Where the stone held, it held narrowly; where it didn't, dark water lay with no bottom visible and no way around. Beneath the causeway sat a vault — the company had come because of that vault — but it was drowned now, and the mechanism that once drained it had seized with decades of rust and sediment. There was a man in the nearest settlement who had worked that mechanism in his youth, or said he had. He would not come for gratitude. He came for silver, and the company paid it without haggling, because the hour was late and the season was not with them, and the thing in that vault would not wait for thrift. The mechanism, once persuaded, groaned and gave.

Then came the vault itself.

It is one thing to want a thing, and another to sit in cold and dark and wait for old water to finish draining, with the smell of wet stone and the sounds the earth makes when it is unhappy with what it is being asked to give up. What was in that vault did not stir with gladness at being found. The company did not rush; they did not turn for the door at the first of it pressing on them. They had come too far and paid too much, and so they held. They waited out what needed waiting, held their ground against what tested it, and by the time the water was low enough to walk in, they had already settled in their minds that they were going to walk.

They came home through the wardens' gate a second time. The wardens nodded them through. The man who had worked the mechanism was already spending his silver in the settlement behind them, and the company came up the road with what they had gone for, and the marsh lay quiet at their backs.

There was a crossing the compact needed, and the party went to make it.

The way led through a tract of drowned ground — reed and black water, the causeways half-sunk and the whole of it watched over by something old that did not sleep. The party knew this. They had come prepared to move quietly, to hug the reed-line and keep their feet from the open mud and give the watching nothing to fix upon. This was the plan they had agreed upon before they set out: that you did not bargain with what kept that place, and you did not fight it either; you slipped past while its attention lay elsewhere.

It did not lay elsewhere. One of them stepped wrong — a foot planted where the mud gave a sound it should not have given — and the attention came around to them all at once. Not a sound, not a shape, only the weight of a gaze that pressed on the back of the neck and turned the reeds into walls. They froze where they stood, and the silence told them clearly that they had been found. The plan they had come with was finished.

So they stood in the open water with nowhere to go, and they spoke among themselves in low voices, and they understood what the crossing wanted from them. It wanted acknowledgment. It wanted to be paid.

The one who carried the thing of most worth to them — a worked piece they had meant to bring home — laid it down in the mud at the edge of the open water. Pressed it flat beneath both palms and held it there, not casting it away but offering it: a deliberate giving, witnessed by the others. The reed-line had gone utterly still. When the hands came up empty the weight lifted, the gaze shifted off, and the way through opened the way a door opens when the right key is turned.

They crossed. They came to what they had gone for and they brought it back. The thing given stayed behind in the mud, which is where such things go, and the crossing let them pass without further trouble in either direction.

That is what it cost them, and they paid it without hesitation, which is the part worth remembering.
