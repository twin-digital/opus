# Of the Expedition to the Buried Reach, and What Befell Those Who Sought Its Depths

The covenant had long kept word of the buried reach — that place where an older structure lay swallowed beneath the silt and encroaching water of the low country, sealed since the age of the second contraction. What the covenant sought within it is a matter the senior testimonies do not record with precision; what is certain is that a party departed in early autumn, when the water table permitted passage through the lower approaches, and that they returned within the fortnight having gained nothing.

They crossed the outer margin in silence, for silence was the only warrant they possessed. The wardens of that ground — those whose families have kept watch over the sunken causeway for as many generations as anyone troubles to count — were known to turn back strangers, and a covenant party carrying tools of opening would not have been received as pilgrims. They went therefore by the seep-marsh on the eastern side, where the ground exhales its cold breath even in summer and the reeds stand tall enough to swallow a person from the waist down. Moving by night, reading stars through cloud, they pressed through the mire without a light to betray them, and the wardens heard nothing. The outer approach was won.

That silence earned them only the threshold. Within the first great chamber — where a collapsed gallery had opened a wound in the old stonework wide enough to step through — the party found the reach already claimed. A company of salvagers, rough-handed men and women who had followed the same rumor by a different road, had made the place their camp. There was no parley in them; they came up from their fire with iron already moving, and what followed was brief and without ceremony. The covenant party pressed the attack with a cold efficiency that left three of the salvagers unable to continue and the rest scattered into the outer dark. The chamber was held.

Here the record bends, and what follows is the crux of the matter. The deeper room — the one the salvagers had evidently been working toward, if the direction of their digging meant what it seemed to mean — proved to be an antechamber sealed by a system of interlocking stone tablets, each inscribed in a hand so old its conventions had drifted far from anything still in common use. The chronicle draws here from the testimony of the party's scholar, and it is a painful account: hours spent before those tablets, tracing the root forms through their probable derivations, cross-referencing the symbols against every cognate that could be recalled. The language was not wholly unknown — its bones were recognizable — but the surviving texts above ground were liturgical, the language of the altar, and what was written here was technical, the language of builders and mechanists. The vocabulary of construction and sequence had no surviving gloss. What the tablets demanded of those who wished to pass, the scholar could not determine with enough certainty to act upon; to guess wrongly was to risk consequences the tablets themselves suggested were severe.

And so the party turned to the stone itself. Each tablet was the weight of two grown men, seated in channels worn smooth by their designers. The party braced and strained and attempted every angle of leverage the narrow space permitted, working in shifts until their shoulders ached and the cold of the lower chamber had settled into their joints. Whether the mechanism required a particular sequence the tablets had been meant to communicate, or whether some element of its design lay hidden from sight, they could not determine — but the tablets did not yield. The reach refused them, as it had refused, in all likelihood, many before.

They withdrew before the autumn rains could swell the water table and seal the lower passages. The buried reach kept what it held, and the covenant recorded the attempt without embellishment.

# Chronicle of the Causeway Vault Expedition

The company went out in the cooling season, when the long grasses of the approach road had begun to yellow and the sky carried that high, pale quality that presages a hard winter. Their errand lay in the interior of the disputed country — the low wetlands where an old causeway, half-swallowed by the marsh, was said to conceal beneath its foundations a vault of the early builders.

The first obstacle was not the marsh itself but the people who tended its borders: a council of elder landholders who had watched covenant expeditions pass through their country and return with whatever the country had yielded, leaving behind only trampled crops and the indifferent courtesy of travellers who have gotten what they came for. The council had accordingly posted wardens at the only dry road in, and closed it to all who could not provide satisfactory account of themselves.

The company's leader did not demand passage, nor attempt to move around the blockade. She sought formal audience with the council and spoke plainly to what the covenant hoped to find and what it would require of the land to find it. She acknowledged the grievances that had been laid before covenant parties in the past and gave undertakings about the conduct of her company that the council could hold her to. She spoke for the better part of a morning. When she was done, the wardens stepped aside, and the dry road lay open.

But the vault itself proved more difficult to access than the road, for the causeway had settled badly over the centuries, and the entrance to what lay beneath it had been sealed — not by design, but by the slow weight of the marsh pressing inward. The builders who knew that country best were a family of salvagers who worked the drowned structures for usable stone and salvageable ironwork; they alone possessed the tools, the knowledge, and the boats sufficient to break the seal and shore the passage against collapse. They had no reason to offer this service cheaply, and they did not. The company spent coin it had not planned to spend, and the agreement was settled on the salvagers' terms rather than their own. Therefore the vault opened.

What waited inside was not danger but patience's demand. The vault had been used, in some forgotten generation, as a place to store things against flooding: documents wrapped in oilcloth, small objects of uncertain purpose packed in sealed jars, crates of materials that had absorbed the damp over centuries until their original nature was no longer legible. The company worked through it methodically, without haste, without the sense that any single object would announce itself as the thing worth the journey. The salvagers had given them two days; they used them both. The cold came up through the stone of the floor and into their boots, and the light they worked by guttered in the bad air, and there were hours when the effort seemed to promise nothing at all — but the company did not allow that hollowness to govern them, and they finished what they had come to do.

They returned along the dry road at dusk on the second day, the wardens nodding them through without ceremony. It had been, in the end, a quiet expedition — no siege, no contest of arms, nothing to fill a more martial sort of account. Yet the willingness to speak honestly before those whose land they walked, the readiness to pay what a thing was worth, the composure that holds when cold and dark and fruitless labour press in from all sides: these had been instruments as real as iron, and in that season, sufficient.

# From the Covenant Chronicles: The Passage of the Reed-Fen Company

There are expeditions remembered not for their ease but for the quality of their turning — those which, caught between the world's refusal and the covenant's need, found the one door that opened and had the courage to walk through it. The company that pressed into the drowned margins east of the causeway in the late season belongs to this reckoning. What follows is set down from the testimonies and from what the fen itself, in its way, left behind.

Their intention, at the outset, was concealment. The wardens of that place — half-seen things that move through standing water as wind moves through reeds, old guardians of a very old arrangement — are not easily faced, and the party knew it. They chose instead to go unseen, picking their way across the submerged causeways by night, reading the channels with the care of those who have studied such passages before. They were learned enough in the craft of it. But the fen is older than craft. The wardens perceived them — perceived, perhaps, the very intention of concealment, which in such places carries its own disturbance — and the company found themselves at last in the open shallows with no cover left and the slow encirclement closing in. Evasion had failed them. Not through any failure of skill, but because the ground itself had disclosed their presence, and skill cannot outwit an environment that does not consent to be deceived.

This is where lesser parties have turned back, or worse. The company did not turn back.

What followed required a different accounting of the self. Among those they carried was something whose worth lay precisely in its singularity — an object long held by the covenant's keeping, a relic of an earlier passage through the same dark margins, freighted with what the fen had once cost and not forgotten. They brought it to the water's edge and offered it back. Not thrown, not abandoned — offered, with the deliberate grammar of a gift between powers that know each other. The youngest of them waded in to the waist and held it under the black surface with both hands until the current, where there should have been no current, took it from below.

The wardens withdrew. The encirclement came apart. Whether this constituted a payment, a recognition, or some older form of covenant-renewal is a matter for those who traffic in such questions; the chronicles are not the place for that disputation. What is recorded is this: the way through the fen was open after, and the company passed, and the expedition resolved as such expeditions rarely do — with the party whole, the purpose achieved, and a lesson written in the mud of the shallows that the covenant has not forgotten.

They had gone in believing that the great virtue of the passage was invisibility. They came out knowing that the fen demands acknowledgment, not concealment; that what it had taken before, it would take again, and that the only honest passage is one where the cost is met in full and openly. The company that gave back what the fen was owed found the reeds parting before them. It is not, perhaps, the lesson the chronicler would have chosen. But the fen does not consult the chronicler.
