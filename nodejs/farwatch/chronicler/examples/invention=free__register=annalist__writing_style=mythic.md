# Of the Descent into the Vault of Ordenmere, and What Came of It

The company set out in the last grey hours before dawn, when the sentinels of the outer approach — those pale, eyeless things that the Aldavrani had posted when the vault was still a working sanctuary — kept their vigil at the far end of the causeway. It was Tessaly who first marked their pattern: three circuits of the broken colonnade, then a long stillness at the southern gate, then three circuits again. Time enough, she judged, for a careful crossing.

They went in single file over the rubble-strewn approach, moving in the silences between the sentinels' turns, pressing flat against the leaning pillars when the pale things reversed their course. Not a word was spoken. Not a boot-sole scraped stone. When the last of the company slipped through the gate and into the shadow of the outer hall, Tessaly let out a long breath, quiet as snowfall, and that was the whole of their celebration.

The inner hall proved less accommodating. Whatever the old Aldavrani had originally set to guard their reliquary, the vault's present tenants were not those. Three of the deep-chained things that haunt such ruins had come to settle here, drawn by the lingering resonance of old sanctity, which to their kind is as a lamp to moths. They came out of the dark between the pillars without preamble, and the company met them in the narrow space where the hall collapsed to half its width — ground they had chosen for exactly this event. The fight was close and hard and short. They drove the creatures back against the rubble, denied them the room to spread, and finished them in the confines where superior numbers count for little. When it was done the company bound their wounds by the light of a shielded lamp and said little, which is the way of such things.

Then came the vault door itself. And here the account turns.

The door was old — older than the Aldavrani who had built above it, older than the naming of the hills. What stood in the wall was not stone in the ordinary sense but something that had been made stone by deliberate art, set with twelve bands of inscription running from floor to lintel in a script that none of their references had prepared them for. Maren had carried the tablets — three rubbed copies of the known Ordenmere texts, wrapped in oilcloth against the damp — and she laid them out on the vault floor and worked at the door's inscription for the better part of an hour. The letters shared shapes with the known script but the underlying grammar refused her. Whatever the builders had sealed in — or sealed out — they had not trusted the words of their own public records to do the work. A deeper language governed the door, and Maren did not have it. She said so plainly, rolled the oilcloth around her tablets, and that was that.

They might have withdrawn then, and a more cautious company would have. But the vault had taken two days' travel and one hard fight to reach, and there was a feeling among them — not spoken, only present, the way weather is present before it breaks — that to stand before the door and turn back without having tried everything was not a thing to be easily lived with.

So they tried the door by force.

Orrec was the strongest of them and he brought his bar to it first, setting the iron into the hairline seam at the door's edge and leaning his full weight behind it. Nothing. The seam did not breathe, did not shift, did not yield so much as a splinter of promise. Two others added their arms to his and they heaved in concert until the bar itself bent out of true. The door stood as it had stood. Whatever craft had made it stone had made it also beyond the reach of their arms, and in the end Orrec set the bent bar down and looked at his hands and said nothing.

They came home without what they had gone for.

The chronicle sets this down not as disgrace but as the plain shape of what befell them. The outer wards were passed. The defenders were overcome. At the door itself the expedition spent all it had — the learning gathered over months of preparation, and the strength of its best arms — and the door was unmoved. The vault of Ordenmere remains sealed. The key to it, if there is a key, lies somewhere in what the company does not yet know.

That is where the matter stands.

# Of the Company's Passage to the Orvaine Reach, Set Down in the Season of Long Rains

The company that rode out under Aldric Vayne's keeping was seven souls, and they carried with them no great arms — only writs of covenant and the particular patience of those who have learned that the world grants passage more readily to the reasonable than to the bold. Their charge was the Orvaine Reach, that wedge of contested highland where the covenant's old claim had grown cold for want of anyone troubling to go there. The road was not difficult in itself. What stood upon it was.

Lord Sevain Kreth was what stood upon it.

He held the lowland passes with inherited right and recent violence, and the rumour that preceded his name was not kind. Lesser companies had gone around, adding three weeks to their journey for the sake of avoiding him, and had arrived diminished. Vayne chose otherwise. He sent riders ahead with the covenant's seal and a request, courteously worded, for an audience — not passage, but audience — and when the answer came back that Kreth would hear them, the company rode into the fort at Callsmouth with their weapons sheathed and their faces composed.

What passed in that hall between Vayne and Kreth is a matter of some dispute among those who were not in it, which is to say most people. What is not disputed is that Vayne spoke for two hours without raising his voice, that he made no threat and offered no bribe and claimed no precedence over the lord's own dignity, and that he set before Kreth, with a cartographer's precision, the exact nature of the covenant's interest in the Reach and the exact nature of Kreth's own interest in remaining at peace with the covenant's friends. He did not argue. He explained. There is a kind of explanation that feels, to those who receive it, like the slow arrival of an obvious thing they had always nearly known, and whatever Vayne laid out in that hall worked upon Kreth in that fashion. By nightfall the company had a letter in the lord's hand, sealed and countersigned, granting them free movement through the passes for so long as their errand required. They slept under Kreth's roof and departed before the dew had lifted.

The passes themselves were passable, but the Greyfen — the broad, sluggish river that drained the eastern slopes — was not, or not easily. The seasonal rains had fattened it past the fords and taken out the rope-bridge at Henwode Crossing, leaving only the ferryman at the village of Suthe, a man named Corvin Ault who had positioned himself, with the unapologetic confidence of a man who knows he is singular, as the sole viable crossing in forty miles. His fee was not modest. It was not even honest. It was the fee of a man who understood that those who needed to cross would pay what he asked, and who had accordingly asked for a great deal.

Vayne paid it. He counted out coin into Corvin Ault's palm without argument or theatre, and the ferryman, perhaps surprised to be denied the usual negotiation, ferried them across the Greyfen in four trips, grudging but prompt. The coin came from the expedition's reserve, which had been assembled precisely for necessities of this kind, and the crossing was made whole, and the company dried their boots on the eastern bank and continued.

What awaited them in the Reach was not an enemy. It was time, and cold, and the particular grinding quality of a task that will not be hurried.

The covenant's claim rested on the occupation of the Orvaine cairns — a chain of old boundary markers across the highland plateau — for thirty unbroken days. This was the law, not the covenant's law but the old charter law, the law that Kreth himself acknowledged. Thirty days of presence, recorded and witnessed. The company established camp among the first cairns and began to wait.

The rains came in earnest. The plateau, which had looked merely austere in the lowland accounts, proved to be a place of considerable character in wet weather — the wind had opinions, the ground refused to dry, and the nearest wood for burning lay two hours' descent below them. Morale is a substance that erodes in such conditions without any single dramatic cause, and the chronicler will not pretend it did not erode. There were nights when the question of why this mattered, this distant highland claim, this cold plateau, these ancient stones — that question took on the kind of weight that is easiest to lift in daylight and grows heavier past midnight.

The company held. That is the plain fact of it. They organized themselves with the methodical care of people who have decided that the keeping of routine is itself a form of resistance against the formlessness that weather and isolation conspire to produce. Watches were kept. The register was written in every morning. The cairns were walked each afternoon regardless of what the sky was doing. When Fereth Osse, who had been the most skeptical of the venture from its beginning, came to Vayne on the twenty-second night and said that the claim was not worth what it was costing them, Vayne listened to him fully before answering, which was the answer, or most of it.

On the thirtieth morning the register was closed and the count was done. The company descended from the Orvaine plateau in better weather than they had earned the right to expect, crossed the Greyfen at a southern ford that the rains had at last relinquished, and rode home through Kreth's country — where the lord's letter, still in Vayne's coat, would not be needed — carrying with them what they had gone for.

# The Account of Vel Carath, in the Fourth Year of the Autumn Reckoning

The company set out from the Thorngate crossing on the third morning after the autumn count, bound for the Ossuary of Vel Carath — that old wound in the hills where the covenant's founders once buried what they could not keep and could not destroy. The errand was recovery: a specific thing, set down in the archivist's sealed instructions, and of the four who made the march only Mira Ashbound had read them, she being their warden and keeper of such knowledge. She kept her own counsel about what lay waiting.

They made the foothills by midday of the second march and came upon the outer approaches of Vel Carath as the light was thickening toward evening. The Ossuary sits within a hollow of pale stone, a depression carved by old waters and ringed by pillars of calcified root that the long-dead planted to mark the bounds of sanctified ground. Within that ring, nothing grows. The soil is grey as ash, and the air above it sits still even when the ridge-wind runs hard across the surrounding hills. Those who have been there will tell you it is not a pleasant stillness.

The approach required passing the Warden of the Threshold — that is the name given in the covenant's older records to the presence that inhabits the outer ring. Whether it is a bound spirit, a lingering echo of old consecration, or something less classifiable, the records do not agree. What was known: it could be avoided. Other companies had done it. The thing moved in long unhurried circuits of the outer ring, and there were gaps in its passage wide enough for a careful party to slip through unseen.

They had studied those patterns. Elowen Darkwater had mapped the timing from three prior expeditions and held her figures to be sound. The party chose their gap, took positions at the break in the outer pillars, and waited in good silence for the moment to open.

It did not open as they had reckoned.

Whether the Warden's circuit had shifted in the seasons since those accounts were written, or whether some quality of the autumn evening had compressed the gap in ways that Elowen's numbers could not have anticipated, the moment that should have been clear was not. They had committed before they understood this — already inside the ring when the presence turned and took them in its attention. The party described it afterward as the sensation of being read, each of them in turn, like a page held up to a lamp. There was nowhere to be small. The way to press forward was closed, and the way behind was no longer theirs to walk without consequence.

It was Mira Ashbound who understood first what was required, and who moved without consulting the others, as though she had always known the errand might arrive at precisely this juncture. From the inner pocket of her coat she drew out the bone-handled seal she had carried for eleven years — the token of her full standing as warden, the physical proof of everything she had earned and been given authority to act upon. She set it on the grey soil at the center of the ring, pressed both hands flat to the ground beside it, and spoke the words of formal relinquishment in a voice level enough to carry without traveling. Not a trade. Not a petition. A giving without condition, offered before a presence old enough to distinguish the difference, and to weigh it accordingly.

The Warden held them a long moment after she finished.

Then it withdrew its attention, and the hollow was still again — differently still, the way a room is still after someone has departed it rather than the way it is still while they wait. The way forward lay open. The company went in, completed what they had come to do, and came home.

Mira Ashbound walked back through the Thorngate without the seal and bore that plainly, as a settled thing. She said nothing about it that has been committed to any record. The covenant logged the mission complete, and made no further inquiry into the cost.
