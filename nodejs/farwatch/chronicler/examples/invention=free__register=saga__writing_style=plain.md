# The Account of the Thornwater Expedition

In the third week of the autumn rains, a company of five set out from Varek's Hold for the old watchtower at Thornwater, where the Sealed Archive was said to lie. The archive had been shut since the Burning, three generations past, when the compact's founders had hidden there what they could not carry and locked the door against an enemy who never came to test it. What lay inside was no longer known by name — only that it had been worth hiding, and that no one since had managed to open it.

The company was led by Marta Caldek, who had twice wintered in those hills. She brought Oren, who had once served at the tower and remembered its duty-rotations; Liss and her brother Tal; and an old reader named Brevik, who was said to know the scripts of the founders' age.

They reached the watchtower before dawn, in hard rain that covered sound. Oren led them through a gap in the southern wall where the mortar had crumbled and never been repaired — he had noted it in his service years and trusted the garrison's neglect. The sentries were sheltered at the tower's base. The company moved along the inner wall, keeping low, and reached the archive stair without raising a sound.

The stair's foot held a guard-post. Two soldiers who had drawn the worst watch of the season sat hunched over a brazier. Marta had hoped to pass them as Oren had passed the outer wall, but one turned at the wrong moment, and there was no longer any use for quiet words or borrowed pretexts. What followed was brief: Liss and Tal took the guards before either could reach the bell-cord. Marta pulled the post's door shut and barred it from within.

They were inside. The garrison did not yet know it.

The sealed door waited at the archive's end, set into the living rock — iron-bound oak three hands thick, with a mechanism at its center: a disc of bronze set with symbols in the founders' tongue. Brevik studied it by lamplight for the better part of an hour. He knew the script well enough; that much proved true. But the symbols on the disc were not liturgical, and not administrative. They were something older — a keying system from before the Burning, whose inner logic answered to no text Brevik had read or memorized. He tried four sequences of the disc. None moved it. He sat back and said that he needed books he had not brought, and that without them he was working in the dark.

Oren and Tal did not accept this quietly. They levered a bar from the stair's railing into the mechanism's housing and threw their weight against it. The housing held. They found a loose stone in the floor and used it as a hammer against the disc's edge, hoping to jar the tumblers into position. The disc bent and would not turn at all after that. Whether it had been one position away from opening, none could say, and Brevik did not wish to speculate.

There was noise above before long — a sentry completing his round, or the watch changing; it was impossible to tell which. The sealed door was behind them and the stairs were ahead of them, and the thing they had come for was still in the rock. Marta made the only choice that remained. They went back through the crumbled wall and out into the rain.

They returned to Varek's Hold with nothing they had not carried out. When pressed, Brevik said that if the mechanism had not been bent he might have read it, given time and the right texts. Whether that was true is a harder question than the old reader chose to sit with.

The archive at Thornwater remained sealed.

# The Opening of Thorngate

The compact sent three of its own to Thorngate in the forty-second year of the Accord, when the road through the Ash Fells had been shut these two seasons running by Cassel Thorne's decree. The compact needed that road — the eastern settlements were going without iron and grain — and therefore the masters chose Aldred Mace, Isbet Carra, and the one they called Wren to carry their case to Thorne's hall.

Thorne was a man who had built his name on the memory of a slight, and it was known he had turned away three petitioners before the last harvest was done. Aldred Mace had sat across tables from harder men, and he did not come with demands. He came instead with a grievance of Thorne's own: the matter of the Orveld merchants who had grazed their animals on Thorne's lower pastures without leave, the season before the road was closed. Aldred named this plainly, acknowledged the insult on the compact's behalf, and asked to speak to what restitution might look like. Thorne had not expected to be heard before he spoke, and so when he did speak he was less harsh than he had meant to be. By the time the evening meal was done, Thorne had agreed to receive the compact's full proposal in the morning.

What remained was the price. Thorne set it high — not from need, but to learn whether the compact thought the road worth the cost. Isbet Carra, who held the compact's purse on this journey, did not flinch and did not bargain. She set the coin on the table piece by piece, each piece saying the same thing: the compact had already decided, and Thorne could have no doubt of it. He pocketed the payment and pressed his seal to the paper.

The road, however, was not yet open. Thorne's reeve, a man named Colm, had governed the closed road for two seasons and had done well by it — certain merchants who moved goods by the longer southern pass had paid him for the arrangement, and he was not eager to see it end. He delayed the lifting of the decree on one pretext, then another: surveyors needed to inspect the road's condition; certain tolls had not been formally assessed; the order required copying before it could be posted. Wren and her companions waited at Thorngate without complaint. They did not press Colm, and they did not threaten him, and they did not offer him coin. They had the seal and they had the paper, and they gave Colm nothing that let him say afterward that the compact's people had forced his hand. On the fourth morning the pretext ran out, and the decree was lifted.

The party returned before the month was done, the road behind them open.

# The Thorngate Account

The party chose to move through the Thorngate before dawn, on the belief that what kept the gate slept in the cold hours. This was not true.

They were within the stones — the narrow corridor where the old walls press to arm's length apart — when the way ahead went wrong. The air thickened. The gate-ward, which the old accounts name as Osset of the Standing Eye, came into the passage before them, and it did not come quickly, which was the sign that it was not alarmed. It had known they were coming.

The gate would not be passed, and to force it would have cost more than the expedition was worth. But Tauren Brek, the compact's eldest field-hand, had walked into standing places before. He knew the grammar of them. He told the others to be still, and he stepped forward, and he cut his palm on the edge of his own belt-knife — a thing that costs nothing to say and something real to do — and he pressed the bleeding hand flat against the foundation stone of the left wall and held it there until the stone was warm.

Osset stood a moment. Then it withdrew.

The party came through the Thorngate and reached what they had gone to reach, and they brought word of it home. The gate-ward has their measure now, and they have its name.
