The company set out on the third morning after the rains broke, when the ground was still soft but the mist had thinned enough to see the ridge. They had waited on that mist — had counted on it, indeed — for the watchers on the eastern approach keep their fires close and their ears sharp, and a party moving in numbers across open ground would not pass unnoted in clear weather. The mist served, and they served themselves well within it: they picked a path in silence through the birch-brake above the old road, passing close enough to the watch-post that one of the sentinels' lanterns could be seen swinging through the vapour not forty yards off. No alarm was raised. They were inside the perimeter before the light began to lift.

What waited for them past the first gate was another matter. The garrison — if it can be called that, three quarters of it being something other than living soldiers — had not left the inner court undefended, and the company understood from the moment they came over the wall that the way forward would cost them. It did. The fighting was close and ugly, conducted in poor light across ground slick with old water, and before it was done two of the company were carrying wounds that would slow them on the return. But the court was theirs when it ended, and the way to the lower passages stood open.

The lower passages were where the expedition came apart.

The vault beneath the great span is old — older by some centuries than the structures raised atop it, old enough that the script carved into its inner lintels belongs to no tradition the company could set against anything they knew. They had brought a man who reads three hands of the old forms, and he spent the better part of an hour moving between the carvings with a lamp, tracing the characters, working through what permutations he could reach. He could not reach enough. The key — if a key was what it was — remained locked in the stone. The seals on the inner door stood as they had stood, and the door did not move.

That was the moment the expedition should have turned back. It did not, because the door was there, and the company had bled for the court above, and the mind persuades itself that what lies behind a door must justify what it cost to reach it. Therefore they tried the door as a door: they put their shoulders to it and their weight against it, set a bar beneath the lower edge and heaved. The stone had been seated for centuries. It did not give, and what flexed instead was the bar, and the back of the man who had already taken a wound above. They stopped when it was clear they would pull the arch down on themselves before they would shift the seal.

The company came home without what they had gone for, bearing men who needed the surgeon's attention before they needed anything else. The vault beneath the causeway kept its own counsel. It keeps it still.

# Of the Company's Eastern Passage

The company came down from the ridge road in the last hour of afternoon, when the light had gone copper and the valley below lay in the uncertain haze that belongs to neither day nor evening. The settlement at the river's bend — where the marsh-wardens kept their winter quarter and their opinions of outsiders — had been described to them as unwelcoming to those who arrived unasked. This was known before they set out. The company had prepared accordingly.

Their speaker went forward alone. She carried no visible weapon, and she carried also the better part of an hour's patience, which proved the more necessary of the two. The warden-captain was not a suspicious man by temperament — only by practice, which is a different thing and harder to move. He had seen enough of what drifted down from the uplands to have formed his conclusions, and he had formed them carefully. She addressed those conclusions directly: she named the families she had dealt with in the eastern parishes, the road she had come by, and the names of certain dead men whose reputations in that country still warranted trust. She asked only that the company be permitted to cross at the warden-post and go east into the fen. The captain listened. He put three questions to her, each practical, and received three practical answers. He let them cross.

Therefore they stood on the far bank before dark, and the question became not whether they could proceed, but how.

There was a ferryman — though the word dignifies, somewhat, the man who worked the flat-bottomed skiff that served as the only crossing for twenty miles in either direction. He was old and unhelpful and entirely unmoved by urgency or courtesy, and he named a price plainly intended to end the conversation. The company's quartermaster had long since learned that certain obstacles wear many expressions but only one face beneath them all. He met the price without argument, counted it out in full, and laid a third part again on top of it — coin of a weight the ferryman could not easily mistake for anything other than what it was. The old man looked at it. He made the skiff ready.

But the crossing was not the last difficulty the country had prepared for them.

Beyond the fen, where the ground firmed and the sedge gave way to older and harder terrain, the road climbed into two days of the sort of going that does not announce itself as arduous until you have been in it some hours. The cold came in from the northeast the second night — not the killing cold, but the patient kind that finds every seam and loosened button and lies in wait there. They had one tent between them adequate to such weather, and they took turns sleeping nearest the outer edge of it, and no one slept well, and the third morning broke grey and wet and without encouragement.

The question that settles over a company in such hours is rarely spoken aloud, but it is present in the way people move and in the silences between them. They had come far enough that turning back would cost nearly as much as going forward. What lay ahead was known to none of them by direct experience — only by report, and reports are always more comfortable than the ground they describe. The rain found the collars of coats and the laces of boots and every other place it was not wanted.

They struck camp and went forward. There is little more to say of it than that. They moved through the wet morning and the grey afternoon, and toward evening the country ahead opened, and the place they had come for rose on the low ridge before them in the failing light. The company came into it wearied and soaked and sound, and what they had gone so far to find, they found.

# The Warden's Crossing

The company had made good time through the drowned reaches — three days of wading and portage, the water black with old rot and the reeds tall enough to swallow a man standing — and by the fourth morning they stood at the outer margin of the warded causeway, where an unsleeping eye has been kept on the road between the marsh and the high ground since before any living warden first took up the charge.

The plan had been to go by night through the shallows north of the causeway, where the bank shelves and the rushes grow thick enough to swallow a file of people moving slowly and without light. They went carefully — boots stripped off and bundled, no fire, no speech, nothing but the careful pressing of feet into silt and the small sounds of water yielding. For a time it seemed the crossing would hold. But the warden that night was not watching the road. The warden was watching the water. Something in the party's passage stirred what lay beneath — the cold that clings to deep places, or the mud remembering its disturbance — and a lamp bloomed on the causeway above, and a voice carried out across the reeds with the flat authority of one who has said _stop and name yourselves_ many times and been obeyed.

There was a moment then, knee-deep in black water with torches brightening the bank and figures moving to cut off the far shore, when the choices were few and none of them good. To run was to lose half the company in the dark. To fight was to make enemies of the only people who knew the road home, in the only country through which that road ran.

So they waded to the near bank and they named themselves plainly and they stood before the warden in the torchlight with wet feet and the particular expression of people who have been caught doing exactly what they were doing.

It was the eldest among them who grasped what the moment required. The warden held the crossing by old compact, and old compacts have their own currency — not silver, which any traveler might offer, but the thing that is actually owed: an accounting. The eldest reached into the oilskin packet carried since the expedition's first morning, the one no one had opened, the one that held the full record of what lay in the drowned vault below the marsh — the sketches, the measurements, the precise account of the passages and what remained in them. Gathered at no small cost in days and cold water and the labor of careful hands held steady in the dark.

The eldest pressed the packet into the warden's hands. Not as a bribe, which would have been an insult. As tribute. As the thing that a keeper of roads is owed by those who pass through them bearing knowledge: a share of what was found.

The warden studied the pages in torchlight without speaking. The company stood and let themselves be studied along with the pages. At last the warden rolled the packet closed, held it a moment as one holds a thing whose weight has been properly reckoned, and said: _pass, and keep to the high road from the second bridge._

They kept to the high road from the second bridge. By midmorning they had come down out of the marsh entirely, the reeds behind them and the smell of standing water giving way to dry ground and the particular lightness of a homeward road. The company walked it in a silence that was not unhappy — the quiet of people who had given something real and found the exchange, in the end, a fair one.
