The party reached the Irongate Shelf two hours before dawn, when the sentinels of the Pale Watch kept their longest circuits. Maret had timed it from three nights of cold observation on the ridge below: there was a window of six minutes between the east and west patrols, and it opened at the third bell.

They made it count. Single file across the exposed ledge, boots wrapped in oiled cloth — Maret first, then Dav and Ossian, Renn last with the rope coiled at her shoulder. The sentinel on the east walk never turned. They were through the outer gate and down the carved stair before his circuit brought him back to that wall.

Below, in the keep's gut, things were less quiet.

The Pale Watch kept four of their number in the lower guardroom — not sentinels but soldiers, awake and armed, playing at tiles by rushlight. Maret had not known this, and there was no going back up the stair without being heard. Dav put his shoulder to the door and they went in hard, and the fight was brief and brutal in the way small-room fights are: bodies close, no room for a backswing, everything decided in the first few seconds. They took cuts, both of them, but the guardroom was theirs.

They bound the Watch and moved on through the lower passage. At its end was the chamber they had come for — the vault where the Archivist Hollin had sealed the Conclave's redistribution records when the old stewardship fell. Getting this far had been six months' work. What stood between them and those records now was a lock-mechanism set into the vault door: a sequence of iron discs, each face marked with the script of the old Conclave, which had to be turned to the correct alignment before the bolts would draw.

Renn had prepared for this. She had spent the previous month with the covenant's lore-keeper, working through what remained of Conclave script in the upper collection. Enough, they had all believed. She set her lamp close to the discs and began, checking each character against the notes she had copied onto vellum, turning and re-turning through arrangement after arrangement.

The script defeated her. Not through ignorance — she could read the characters well enough. The mechanism did not turn on their literal meaning. It turned on something beneath that, a second system layered inside the first, and the covenant's collection had never held the key to it. She worked two hours without a word, and the vault did not open.

By then the sky through the passage's single vent had gone grey. They had perhaps an hour before the next Watch rotation found the four bound soldiers in the guardroom.

Ossian had said nothing through Renn's working. Now he studied the vault door — its frame, the gap where the bolts seated into the cliff face — and decided that if the mechanism would not yield to understanding, it might yield to force. He set a bar into the hinge gap and put his back into it; Dav braced against the opposite wall and took the other end. They were both large men and neither was giving less than everything.

The door did not move. The hinges were set deep into the rock, and the bolts held against all of it. The bar bent before anything else gave.

They climbed the stair in the early light and slipped out through the outer gate while the sentinels were still at the far ends of their circuits. The vault stood intact behind them, and what was sealed inside it remained there.

# The Thornpass Survey

The party set out in the grey of early spring, when the mud on the Aldric road still held footprints a day old. There were six of them, led by Warden Dov Reach — a man who had negotiated the covenant's grain contracts for seven years and knew when to speak and when to let silence work. They carried a sealed letter from the High Seat and enough coin to outfit a modest household through a winter.

The first difficulty came at Thornpass itself, where the Harrath Brotherhood had held the crossing since the previous autumn. They were not bandits, exactly — they had fought for the western lords in the territorial disputes and been given, in lieu of wages, the right to collect tolls at the pass for a term of three years. They were therefore within their rights, but the toll they quoted was a levy designed to deter rather than to collect.

Reach did not haggle over the posted rate. He asked instead to speak with the Brotherhood's factor, a woman called Senne who had their books in her keeping and who understood, therefore, better than the gate-guards did, what the passage of a covenant party was worth over a three-year term. He sat with her in the toll-house through most of a cold morning, drinking thin ale, and laid out the covenant's regular routes: the herb-gathering expeditions in summer, the two autumn provisioning runs, the spring survey teams. He did not threaten to reroute them. He said only that a rate which brought the covenant through once was worth less than one which brought them through a dozen times. Senne had the gate opened before noon.

Three days south, the party reached the hamlet of Escavar, where the covenant held a standing claim to an old wayhouse. The claim was sound on paper, but the family that had been squatting the building for two seasons had put real work into it — a new roof, a repaired well — and felt the equity of their labour entitled them to some recognition. The family's elder, a man whose hands showed he had done the roofing himself, met them in the doorway and did not move aside.

Reach's second, a woman named Calla Ist, handled it. She did not invoke the covenant's title or call for the man to step back. The evening before, she had asked the hamlet's smith what the repairs were worth. Now she counted out that sum and set it on the gatepost between them. The elder looked at the coin for a long moment, then took it and called his family to bring their things. The party slept that night in beds the family had left behind.

The last obstacle was not a person but a condition. The wayhouse had been their intended resupply point, and from it they still had a two-day walk to the flooded quarry in the hill country where the covenant's stoneworkers believed a particular grey limestone could be found below the waterline. The rain that had been building since the second day came down that night and did not stop. The road dissolved into a series of pools joined by mud. One of the party turned an ankle on a submerged root and had to be helped along. They were cold and behind their time, and two members put it plainly: they should turn back and return in summer when the ground was passable.

Reach heard them out. Then he asked how many days' rations remained and whether the ankle was broken or merely swollen. The rations were enough. The ankle would hold. He said they would keep on, not because he was indifferent to their misery but because he had given his word to the stoneworkers, and he said this plainly: the quarry lease came up for renewal in the autumn, and without a confirmed survey the covenant would lose it. There was nothing more to discuss. The party kept on.

The worst of the weather broke the second morning. The quarry was where the old maps said it was, and the limestone lay below the waterline just as the stoneworkers had reckoned. Reach's report, when he returned, was three pages: what they had found, what it had cost, and the route home.

# The Passage of the Sunken Gate

The expedition was three days into the Ashfeld when they came to the gate — a stone arch half-swallowed by black water, its keystone carved with an eye that had been watching longer than the covenant had been keeping records.

They came at night, which was Daven Rusch's idea. The thing that kept the gate — the older testimonies called it the Warden — moved by sound and breath and the warmth a body shed into cold air. It could not be killed; the testimonies were plain on this. But it slept in daylight, or something near enough to sleep that men had passed in daylight before. Night was wrong. Night was Rusch's error, and the others let him make it because he was the one who had read the testimonies, and they trusted reading.

They stripped their gear of anything that rattled. They bound their boots in cloth. They went in single file through black water, knee-deep and cold enough to ache in the joints, and for twenty yards the arch loomed without incident and the eye above it was just stone in the dark. Then Maren Voss's wrapped boot found a submerged branch and she went down hard, one hand striking the water. The sound was not large. It was the sound of a palm against water, and it carried.

The Warden rose from the far side of the arch.

Three members of the expedition turned back and ran, and the water made the running loud, and that left two people standing under the eye of the keystone: Rusch and Voss.

What Rusch did then, he did without discussion. He reached into his pack and found the journal. Not his own — the covenant's business at the Reliquary of Cael Morvaine will be kept in other records than this one — but the journal he had carried from his father's house for eleven years, the one thing he possessed that his father had touched while still living. He opened it in the dark water. He tore the pages out by handfuls.

Then he burned them.

He had a striker. The pages were dry because he had kept them dry through three days of marsh. They caught fast, and in the light of them the Warden was visible on the far side of the arch, and the eye in the keystone was visible, and Maren Voss's face was visible, watching Rusch feed paper to a fire he held in his cupped hands until the last page caught and went and left only a scorched cover floating in the water.

The Warden went down.

Whether it was the heat or the light that satisfied it, or whether it was something else — a thing loved, destroyed without coercion — the chronicler cannot determine with certainty. The older records suggest the Warden had never cared for heat or light, but for the weight of what was freely surrendered. Whatever reckoning it applied, it found the offering sufficient. It sank, and the water closed over where it had stood, and the arch was open.

Rusch and Voss came through. They completed the covenant's business in the Reliquary before dawn and brought back what they had been sent to bring. On the return they collected the three who had fled, waiting a quarter-mile up the bank, and those three said nothing on the long walk home that Rusch chose to answer.
