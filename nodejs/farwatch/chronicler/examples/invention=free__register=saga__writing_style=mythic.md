# From the Chronicle of Expeditions: The Company of Vethara Sind, in the Matter of the Sunken Vault of Ashenmere

In the third year of Compact-Warden Dravan's tenure, when the recovery of the Ashenmere texts had grown from scholarly ambition to urgent need, the Company of Vethara Sind was sent into the drowned quarter of that city to retrieve what the flood-tide and the centuries between them had not consumed. They carried with them a compact seal of passage and the names of three who had entered Ashenmere before and not returned. They went in autumn, when the water ran low enough to expose the upper courses of the archivist's tower, and the mist that rose from the black shallows at morning swallowed the far bank entire.

The tower was held. Not by men — the garrison that had taken root there in the years of contested salvage was no formal force, but rather a loose confederation of scavengers owing allegiance to the broker Caleth Mourne, who had learned early that other men's ignorance of a thing's worth was more profitable than any labor of his own. His people watched the approaches from platforms lashed between the upper windows, and a password was required at the old postern gate, changed each week by Mourne's own hand. The Company could not buy their way past this watch, nor persuade it — Mourne's people had reason to mistrust compact interests, and the history between the Warden's house and the broker was not so short that goodwill might be presumed.

Therefore the Company did not arrive as themselves. Vethara Sind had made study of Mourne's operation across several seasons, and she understood his supply lines. Two of her number had spent the preceding fortnight at the village of Calsten, down the river, where Mourne's provisioners sourced the salt fish and rendered fat that kept his tower people fed through the cold. These two arrived at the postern gate at the hour of the weekly resupply, laden with the right casks, speaking the right names, and bearing a signed receipt in Mourne's own cipher — a copy of a previous receipt, altered in the particulars, the alteration made by a member of the Company who had once forged deeds for a living and still possessed the talent. The watchman at the gate was a careful man, but he was also cold and expecting them, and careful men grow less careful as the expected moment draws near. He passed them through. The others followed in the cart beneath the tarpaulin, as cargo. In this manner the whole of the Company was inside the tower before Mourne's man had set down his lantern.

But a tower with a broker's interest in it is not an empty tower, and the vault below required passage through the inhabited floors. On the second level they were discovered — not by any failure of nerve, but by the chance encounter with a woman who had known Vethara Sind's face from a previous season's dealings and whose memory, unlike the watchman's, had not been dulled by cold or routine. She called out, and the men with her moved. What followed was brief. The Company had not come unprepared for this: they had agreed before the crossing that if discovered, they would not hesitate, and they did not. The fight was short and decisive, the advantage of preparation and position telling against those who had only surprise on their side. When it was done, the way to the vault stair was clear, and the Company descended.

The vault had been sealed when Ashenmere fell, and the seal had held. The door that faced them at the bottom of the stair was a work of the old compact-artificers — three concentric rings of bronze let into the stone, each ring incised with script in the ceremonial register that the archivist-priests of that age had used for binding and warding. The intent of the expedition had always been here: not to force this door, but to read it. The compact archive held partial transcriptions of Ashenmere's vault-seals, and Vethara Sind's scholars had spent months preparing for this reading, confident that with what they carried and what faced them, the door would yield to knowledge.

It would not. The script was not the ceremonial register they had prepared for. Either the transcriptions were wrong, or the vault had been resealed in a different hand after the original archivist's death, or the dialect was older than anyone in the compact had supposed — whatever the cause, the symbols that should have been legible were not. The Company's lore-keeper stood before that door for the better part of two hours, turning pages, setting one lamp after another to the bronze, and came at last to the admission that the knowledge they had brought was not the knowledge the door required. The expedition had been built on a preparation that the reality of Ashenmere had now unmade.

There remained the question of what else could be tried. The door was bronze set into living stone, old work and sound. Vethara Sind was not a woman who accepted a single answer where two remained, and so she set her strongest to it: a sustained effort against the bronze rings themselves, levers worked into the seal's edge, the full weight of those who could be put to the work. The vault had been made to outlast the city, however, and it had outlasted the city, and it outlasted them as well. The rings did not turn. The door did not shift. After a time, the levers bent.

By then the noise of the attempt had traveled. Sounds from above told them that Mourne's surviving people had sent for help, or had found their courage, and the way back up the stair would not long remain as clear as it had been. The Company withdrew in good order, taking their bent levers and their transcriptions that had proved false, and they came out of Ashenmere as the afternoon light was failing, through the shallows and the mist, and across the river to where their boats waited.

They returned to the compact with the vault still sealed, its contents still unknown, and the understanding — now settled and no longer guessable — that the archive's transcriptions of the Ashenmere seals were insufficient for the work. What they had found was the shape of a future attempt's requirements, which is a thing of some value, though it is not the thing they had gone to find.

# The Passage of Eddar's Ford

In the year of the Amber Moot, the company set out under Edric Greave to treat with Ossian of the Thornwall — a man who had held the river crossing at Eddar's Ford for eleven seasons and who had made that holding profitable through the sufferance of no one but himself.

The compact had need of the Ford. The records of the dispute that made it necessary lie elsewhere in these halls; what concerns this account is what was done.

Greave brought three companions and a warrant sealed with the compact's iron mark. He brought also what the occasion required: words chosen with care and a patience that could outlast silence. When they came before Ossian in his hall — a low stone place, fire-lit, smelling of wet wool and cold river — the lord did not rise to greet them, which was understood to mean what it meant. Greave greeted him nevertheless as an equal, named the compact's standing, and laid before him the terms of the proposal: passage rights in full, in exchange for a formal acknowledgment of Ossian's sovereignty over the crossing itself. He neither demanded nor flattered. He presented the arrangement as the sensible conclusion to which two careful men must inevitably come, then fell quiet and waited.

Ossian heard him through without a word. He was a man who had learned that silence costs the listener nothing and the speaker much. Greave had learned the same thing in his own years, and so a long quiet settled over the hall before Ossian said at last that the compact's terms were acceptable in principle, though the price remained to be named.

The price came the following morning, stated plainly over cold bread and river water: a sum in silver that would have provisioned the compact's hall for half a winter. There was no negotiating it; that too was understood. Greave had carried the compact's coin chest in a cart behind the horses, because he had known that Ossian would ask for something real and had judged what that something was likely to be within a margin that proved sufficient. He counted out the silver at the table with neither haste nor ceremony, and the sound of the coins on the wood said what words could not have said as well — that the compact reckoned this worth paying, and paid it without wince. Ossian watched the counting without expression. When it was done he called for his scribe.

The warrant was drawn and sealed before midday. What remained was the return: three days along the river road in weather that had turned cold and mean, the cart now empty, the sealed document wrapped in oilcloth in Greave's saddlebag. On the second night the temperature fell sharply, the road went first to mud and then to something between mud and ice, and one of the horses went lame in the grey hours before dawn. They walked the last day, all four of them, leading the animals by hand. They came to the compact hall at dusk with mud to the knee and the warrant intact.

That is what was accomplished: the Ford opened, the compact sealed, the thing carried home. The compact's hand on the river. What it would yield in the seasons following is a matter for other pages.

# The Passage Through Haerthun's Wound

The expedition had gone to do a thing in the valley called Haerthun's Wound, that long cleft in the hill-country where the stone walls press so close together that noon lasts less than an hour and the rest of the day passes in a cold grey light, and where the Keeper of the Threshold has stood since before the Compact was cut. The compact sent them seven strong; the chronicler does not enumerate them here, for the deed that followed was not divided among them but belonged to all of them as one act.

Their first counsel, taken at the ridge above the valley, was to go in unseen. They had reason to believe the Keeper would demand what they could not give — some proof of standing they did not possess, or a toll in kind they had not carried with them — and so they chose the western approach through the scrubwood, in the hour before the mist lifts and the light comes level, when a careful party that knows the ground can move without seeming to move. They were careful. They knew the ground.

The Keeper was waiting in the path when they came through the last of the scrub, standing in the middle of what had been open passage not an hour before, with the mist at his back and his eyes on them as though he had counted their steps since the ridge. What betrayed them is a matter some in the compact still dispute. Others, who have made the passage since, report that the valley floor has a quality about it — that a footfall on the stone of Haerthun's Wound travels differently than footfalls elsewhere — and that the Keeper has learned in his long years to hear what he cannot see. Whatever the cause, the evasion was over, and the thing they had meant to slip past now stood before them, and the way forward was closed.

Therefore, with no cleverness left to spend, they were made to deal with what they had hoped to avoid.

It was Aelindra Duskmantle who stepped out from the party — not because it fell to her by rank or by lot, but because she saw what the moment was and what it would cost and she did not wait for someone else to see the same. She was the one among them who carried a thing worth giving: the short blade her grandmother had worn into the Thornfield War and out of it again, a knife of no particular make and no particular edge, but one that had been in the Duskmantle house three generations and had passed from dying hands to living ones twice and been carried to vigil once. She drew it from its sheath and drove it point-first into the earth between herself and the Keeper, and she spoke the words of tender — not asking, not bargaining, but relinquishing — and she stepped back.

The Keeper looked at the knife standing in the ground for a long time. Long enough that the mist thinned and the thin noon light came down into the valley. Then he stepped aside, and the way through was open, and he did not take his eyes from Aelindra as the party passed.

The knife did not return with the expedition.

What the party sought in the deep of Haerthun's Wound, they found, and they brought word of it home before the week turned. The expedition was entered in the ledger as accomplished. The compact received what it had sent them to learn.

Aelindra said little of the Keeper, or of her grandmother, afterward. What she carried home from Haerthun's Wound was not a thing she put down anywhere the chronicler can point to; but she came home, and the thing was done, and the way had been bought with what it asked.
