# Of the Expedition to the Sealed Undercroft

The covenant sent four of its number into the low country east of the ridge, where an ancient undercroft lay sealed beneath a causeway long since swallowed by the marsh. What they sought within was plain enough: the lower vault was said to hold records of the original survey, documents that would have settled the boundary dispute then troubling the covenant's eastern holdings. They entered by night.

The causeway was watched — two wardens of the marsh-guild sheltered in a reed-thatched station at the near end, their fire visible a quarter-mile off. Rather than announce themselves and submit to questioning they could not afford, the four went into the water, moving along the causeway's shadow where the planks overhung the reed-beds. They came up on the far side without raising a sound, and the wardens never turned from their fire. The door in the causeway's flank opened before them.

Below, in the drowned dark of the first chamber, something had been left to keep the place. Whether it had once served the builders or had simply moved into the vacancy of years, the record does not say; what the record does say is that it contested passage, and that the four answered that contest at close quarters, and carried it. One of them took a wound in the press, but the thing was put down, and the party went deeper.

The inner vault held what they had come for, or seemed to. The shelves stood intact. The documents were there. But the script was not the one they knew — some older form, angular and close, using ligatures that none of the four could parse. They spent time on it. The attempt was honest, and it came to nothing. The records remained unread on the shelf, as sealed in their meaning as the vault had been in its stone.

It was then that one of them proposed they take the documents by force of transport rather than of understanding: if they could not read the records here, they would carry them home entire and find someone who could. The plan was reasonable, and it failed on a simple fact. The documents had been stored in a chest of dark wood banded with iron, and the chest had been set into a recess in the floor and fixed there — bolted through the base into the stone beneath. What held it, held it absolutely. They worked at it until their hands bled and their backs gave out, and the chest did not move by a finger's breadth. They had no tools for the bolts. They had not thought to bring them.

They came back up through the flooded passage and past the warden's fire as the marsh began to gray with morning, carrying nothing but the knowledge of what lay below and the certain understanding that reaching it would require a different preparation than the one they had made.

<chronicle>
What the records preserve of this expedition is consistent enough that its shape can be set down with some confidence, the accounts of those who returned agreeing on the substance of events if not always their particulars.

The covenant dispatched the company in the grey weeks before the thaw, when the marsh roads held and the border clans had not yet begun their seasonal disputes over the eastern grazing grounds. The object of the expedition lay beyond those holdings — a drowned archive beneath an old causeway, sealed in some prior generation, whose contents the covenant had reason to reach. But no party could cross the grazing grounds without leave from those who wintered there, and the clans had not been well-disposed to covenant business for some years.

The company sought audience rather than attempt a passage by concealment, which speaks to a clear-eyed reading of the ground: the marsh-wardens who kept the border crossings were numerous and knew their country too well to be slipped past, and a party caught in the attempt would have forfeited the clans' goodwill for a generation. The company's leader put before the clan elder a proposition of mutual benefit instead — the covenant would document and formally witness the clans' boundary claims in the land dispute then simmering with their eastern neighbors, a service the elder had sought from other quarters without success. The negotiation occupied the better part of two days. The elder gave passage at the end of it, and gave it with provisions and a guide besides.

The guide brought them to the causeway in four days. What met them there was a different matter: the causeway wardens, nominally servants of no clan, in practice servants of whoever paid them. The archive seal had been placed under their keeping generations back, and they held to their custodianship with the particular stubbornness of men whose only authority is the authority to say no. The company did not argue. They paid the fee the wardens named — a sum that exhausted a meaningful share of what the covenant had provisioned for the journey — and paid it without haggling, which the wardens appeared to regard as the conduct of sensible people. The seal was broken and the descent permitted before the afternoon light failed.

What lay beneath the causeway had been wet for a long time. The air was foul and the timbers had rotted in ways that could not always be anticipated before weight was placed on them. Three members of the company turned back before reaching the lower chambers; the record notes this without reproach, the conditions being what they were. Those who pressed on did so because what they had come for was in the lowest vault or it was nowhere, and the vault offered no easier terms than what it asked. The going required crawling through gaps in partially collapsed brickwork, wading passages where the mud came to the chest, and working by feel through stretches where the lanterns could not be held above the water. The duration underground — the better part of a night and the following morning — and the condition of those who surfaced suggest the cost was not trivial.

They came out with what they had descended to find. The guide was paid and released. The elder's boundary claims were duly recorded. The company returned to the covenant.

The chronicle notes this expedition as one that prospered because those who undertook it understood the difference between what could be negotiated, what had to be purchased, and what could only be endured — and met each in its proper kind.

The company came to the marsh in the hour before dawn, when mist lay thick on the water and the reeds stood still in the grey air. Their object was the vault beneath the old causeway — a place the marsh-wardens had long regarded as their own, patrolled with a regularity that spoke less of duty than of jealousy. The plan was to move along the eastern berm while darkness held, keeping low and silent, trusting the fog.

The fog was not enough.

Whether some branch gave underfoot or the wardens had posted a watch that season at a different station than in years past, the company found themselves caught in the open as the first grey light spread across the water. The wardens stood eight to their four, positioned at both ends of the berm, and there was no retreating into the marsh without revealing what they had come for. They stood exposed, their pretexts stripped away, the vault still locked beneath the causeway stones.

What followed was not what anyone watching might have anticipated. The company's eldest — the one who had led them this far and who knew best what lay below — stepped forward and addressed the wardens plainly. She did not lie, and she did not plead. She told them the nature of the vault and what the party sought within it, and then she placed on the causeway stones the one thing she carried that the wardens would value: the survey-rights to a stretch of the upper marsh that her family had held for three generations. She did not offer them as a bribe. She set them down as what they were — a relinquishment, freely made, with no condition attached.

The wardens conferred at the far end of the berm. Then they opened the gate.

The party descended. When they returned to the surface, the document had already changed hands. They departed without ceremony, the way behind them sealed as firmly as it had ever been.
