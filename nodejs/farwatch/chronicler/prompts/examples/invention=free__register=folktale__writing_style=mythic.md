# The Company of Maret at the Sealing of Keln

The company that went south in the last cold weeks before thaw — seven swords and two of the learned — came back in number but not in spirit, and what they found in the Ossuary of Keln is remembered now not for what they carried home, for they carried home nothing, but for how near they came, and how clearly that nearness taught the covenant what it means to stand at the edge of a thing and be turned back by it.

They went seeking the sealed lower chambers, the ones the old survey maps name only as _the deep that waits_. Maret led them, as she had led the two expeditions before this one — a woman who read rooms better than most scholars read books, and who had learned which kind of silence was permission and which was warning. When her company reached the outer courts of Keln — a gallery of columns still upright against a grey sky, half-drowned in the season's last snow — she read those courts as permission, and she acted on it. The wardens who paced those galleries were a dozen strong, wrapped in furs and the comfortable inattention of men who had found nothing for many years and expected to go on finding nothing. Maret put the company into the column-shadows without a word, only the gesture, and they moved through as a held breath moves — no ring of steel, no crunch of boot, nothing that was not snow and dark and cold. The wardens paced on. Not one looked up. The company came through the outer courts like rumor, and was gone before rumor could spread.

The inner hall was another matter, and Maret knew it would be. Here three of the great hounds kept their long watch — creatures set by the makers in some older age, built of jaw and fury and something that preceded both. There was no walking past these. Maret looked at them and then at her people, and the order she gave was wordless: Denavar and the twins to the flanks, herself to the center, and move together or not at all. What followed was quick and costly in the way that honest fights always are, but the company bore themselves steadily and when it was done the hounds lay still across the cold stone floor, and the company stood over them breathing hard, finding themselves intact, pressing on.

They stood then before the great seal.

It was not a door in any usual sense — no hinge, no latch, no timber or iron, but a face of dark stone set flush into the living rock, carved from lintel to threshold in the old script of the makers. Arossa had spent a full season preparing for this moment. She had laid hands on every manuscript the covenant held that touched the order which had once kept this site, and Fenwick beside her knew three dialects of the old tongue and could read the makers' script well enough to have served, in a lesser ruin, as the only scholar needed. They went to work together, reading aloud in low voices, comparing phrase against phrase, tracking the carved lines from their beginnings through the long columns of text to wherever each line ended. They understood pieces: the shape of a commanding phrase, the grammar of an opening, the marks the carvers had used to indicate that a voice was required and a breath and something offered. But the operative center of the working — the passage that would have told them _how_ the command was to be completed — had been worn over what must have been centuries into smoothness, and what they could read was not enough to reconstruct what they could not. They argued through the better part of an afternoon, and when the light falling from the shafts above began to grey toward dusk they admitted to each other and then to Maret that the seal would not open by learning alone, because they did not know enough, and they were not certain anyone living did.

Maret looked at the door a long time. Then she looked at Denavar.

No order needed giving. They understood her and understood themselves, and they brought what they had left to bring: iron spikes driven with hammers into the carved seams, a timber they had carried in against exactly this possibility, every back and shoulder set to the working. Denavar anchored the timber against the spike-heads and the twins braced behind him and the rest of the company pressed where pressing was possible. The door did not shift. It did not groan. It gave no more sign of feeling their effort than a mountain gives sign of feeling the weather. It stood as it had stood for perhaps a thousand years, and when Denavar's hands came away torn and the timber cracked through at the grain and fell, the door remained, unmarked, unchanged, indifferent to all of it.

So they turned for home.

The covenant receives those who return as it has always received them: with warmth, with food, with the space to speak what they saw without judgment on the seeing. Maret's company had gone quietly where silence was needed, had held the inner hall against what guarded it, had brought two of the covenant's finest scholars to a door that defeated both of them. None of this was nothing. But the lower chambers of Keln remain sealed, and whatever the makers placed there still waits in the dark below that impassive stone, patient in the way that only very old things are patient.

The name Keln goes into the ledger of unfinished business, where it will wait alongside the other names, for another season, another company, another scholar who may carry the piece that Arossa and Fenwick between them could not supply.

# The Account of the Emissary Road

Hear now of the company that went out in the grey of early autumn, three from the covenant and one who guided them, charged with a purpose that two prior embassies had brought home undone. The record names them among the worthy, and so they pass into the chronicle.

Their road led them to the city of Varenthis, which sits at the joining of the Sull and the Dusk rivers and has grown fat on the commerce of both. The covenant required a compact with the Varenthine Arbiters — those four merchants who hold the city's bond-house and with it the right to call any debt in the lowland reaches due. The covenant's debts were not yet dangerous, but winter was coming and the next season's pledge would make them so. Someone had to go and speak plainly before the ink dried into something worse.

The gates of the bond-house were not locked to them. The locked thing was harder: Arbiter Semaine, who kept the eldest chair and whose goodwill the others followed as iron follows a lodestone. Semaine had been wronged once by a covenant factor — or believed she had, which is the same thing in a hall of contracts — and she had let her belief settle into certainty over eleven years of quiet distance. When the company's speaker, a woman named Torrel who had served the covenant's hearth-council since before most of the others were grown, rose to address the Arbiters, she did not defend the old factor, nor deny the wound, nor ask for the wrong to be set aside. She acknowledged it. She named the year, named the sum, and named what it had cost Semaine's house in reputation and in silver. Then she set the covenant's counter-offer on the table, not as a price but as a reckoning — as if the covenant had been keeping the tally all along and had come at last to settle it. Semaine looked at Torrel for a long moment across the polished table. Then she set down her stylus, which she had been holding as a woman holds a blade, and the manner of the room changed. They were admitted. They were heard.

Therefore the harder question could be put to the Arbiters, which was the question of the bond itself — the terms on which the covenant's autumn pledge might be restructured into something that would not break them before spring. Here the Arbiters showed their teeth, as was their right and their nature, for a bond-house profits by its terms, and the terms they proposed would have stripped the covenant's river-rights as surety. What followed was not argument but arithmetic: Torrel's second, a young man called Orvane who had spent his apprenticeship in the covenant's counting-rooms and knew every figure of their holdings to the last bale, laid the covenant's full account before the Arbiters without concealment. He showed what they had. He showed what they would have. He showed what the river-rights were worth in the market as it presently stood, which was less than Semaine imagined, and what they would be worth in three years, which was considerably more. The covenant was offering, Orvane explained with a stillness that passed for confidence and became confidence, a share in something growing, not the salvage of something failing. By the third hour the surety had been moved from the river-rights to a smaller and more sensible pledge, and the terms were written in the Arbiters' own hand.

Therefore one last thing remained, and it was the one trial the ledgers do not record easily.

The compact required a night's waiting while the bond-house scribes prepared the formal instrument. The company lodged at an inn called the Two Bridges, and it was there that the news found them: the covenant's factor in Varenthis — a separate soul, not of their party — had that same evening been heard in the lower taverns speaking ill of Semaine by name, drunk on bad wine and older grudges. By morning it would be known. The compact, not yet sealed, might come undone before the ink was cool.

Torrel sat with this for a time. She did not rush into the night to find the factor and silence him; that would only have made the thing louder. She did not send Orvane to the bond-house to explain, which would have been to confirm the story before anyone had officially heard it. What she did instead was remain. She sent word to Semaine's house that she hoped the Arbiter rested well, that the covenant was grateful for the day's patience, and that she herself would be at the Two Bridges until the instrument was ready, should anything require a word. It was a letter of nothing — a letter that said, in the language of such things: we are still here, we have not run, we do not fear the morning. And then she waited through the night, and Orvane waited with her, and their guide slept as guides do when the work is done.

The instrument was brought at first light. Semaine had signed it herself, which was her prerogative but not her custom. The company sealed it, accepted the bond-house's copy, and rode home.

They rode home with the compact bound and witnessed, the covenant's pledge restructured on terms that would hold through the cold season and the one following, and the river-rights still in covenant hands. They had gone to Varenthis to hold something together that was coming apart, and they had held it. The chronicle marks it as a mission carried through — by the words spoken well, by the accounts laid plain, and by the long patience of a woman who understood that trust, once nearly broken, is not repaired by argument, but by presence.

So let it be set down.

# The Crossing at Varenthal Mere

Hear now of the company that crossed the Mere in the year the ice came early — four souls of the covenant: Maren of the Thornside Chapter, who led them; Dault, who carried the sealed words; old Thessip, who had walked such roads before; and the young one they called Corwin, though that was not the name he had been given. What drew them to Varenthal Mere is a tale for another keeping. That they went, and what befell them there, is the account set down here.

The Mere was not a lake, though the name would have you think so. It was the dead country between the standing stones — a mile of grey water and grey sedge and silence that had weight to it, the kind of silence that listens. The wardens of that place were old. They did not sleep. They had not slept since the first compact was struck, and they would not sleep until the world released them from it. Every company that had ever tried to cross the Mere had known this, and every company that had ever tried to cross the Mere had believed it would not matter.

Maren's company believed the same.

They went in the late afternoon, when the fog came down from the hills and lay across the water like wool, thick enough to muffle footfall, thick enough, they told themselves, to muffle them entire. They moved in single file along the causeway stones — the old way, half-sunken, each step placed with care, no word spoken. Thessip had mapped the route from memory. Corwin watched the fog. Dault held the sealed words against his chest as though warmth could keep them safe.

They made it perhaps two-thirds of the crossing before the water changed.

There is no other word for it. The water changed. The fog did not lift; if anything it thickened. But beneath the grey surface of the Mere, something began to move, and the cold that rose from the water was not the cold of the season. Maren felt it first — she had the gift of such feelings — and she raised her fist for stillness. But stillness was no longer what the Mere required. The wardens had already taken note of them. The stones beneath their feet began, not to tremble, but to announce themselves: each footfall rang out across the water like a struck bell, though they had made no sound a moment before. The fog drew back as if called. And the shapes at the water's edge, which they had taken for sedge-clumps and old posts, turned their faces toward the causeway.

The company had been found. There was no further use in quiet steps.

It was Thessip who understood what was needed, because Thessip had walked such roads before, and had paid attention to what he had learned. He said nothing. He reached into the pack at his side and brought out the thing he had carried all that road — the carved bone figure, old work, the kind that takes a hundred hours and the focus of a grief — which had been his mother's, and before that her father's, and which was the last remaining piece of a line that would now end with him, he having no children and no intention of any. He had known, perhaps, that it might come to this, and had brought it for exactly that reason, which is the kind of knowing that the body carries before the mind will admit it.

He stepped to the edge of the causeway stone and set the figure upon the water.

It did not sink. It sat upon the surface of the Mere the way a leaf will sometimes sit, balanced between the water and the air, belonging to neither. The shapes at the water's edge were still. Old Thessip watched the figure, and his face was a study in the composure of a man who has already done the grieving and now only confirms it. Then the figure tipped, and sank, and the Mere received it without a ripple.

The silence that followed was a different silence than the one before. The water did not warm. The fog did not lift. But the weight of attention lifted from them — the sense of being observed by something that could undo what they were — and in its place came the particular quality of air that a door makes, when it has been opened from the other side.

The company crossed. They did not look back at the water, and they did not speak until the far shore grass was under their feet, and even then only in the low voices of people who are not yet sure that the moment has truly passed.

What Thessip had given, the Mere kept. He carried the memory of it home, which is a different kind of weight — lighter in the hand, heavier in the chest — and the chronicle sets it down here so that those who come after may know the cost of the crossing, and whose it was.

The expedition came home. The sealed words were delivered. The covenant received them back.
