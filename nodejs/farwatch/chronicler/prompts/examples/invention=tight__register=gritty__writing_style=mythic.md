They came to the outer passages in silence, threading the corridors where the watch was thickest, and where another company might have bled for every yard they moved like shadow through shadow until the deep wards lay open before them. The wardens within were wakeful and armed, and the fighting that followed was short and ugly — blades in close quarters, a brief press of bodies in torchlit stone — but when it was done the party stood and the wardens did not. There the way opened onto a sealed gallery whose markings none among them could read: they had seen such ciphers only in partial copies, and what they carried of the tradition proved too small a lantern for so deep a dark, so they stood before the inscription long, argued the grammar of it, and went away no wiser. Therefore they brought what force they had against the sealed passage — iron tools and the weight of shoulders and the slow wrecking labour of people who had run out of cleverness — but the stone was not to be moved by any means they possessed, and in the end they came up from the deep into the open air with nothing to show for their going down.

When the party came to Vareth's Crossing, the lord-warden of that place — a lean man named Sorvais, with the eyes of someone long practiced at reading advantage — received them in his hall with neither warmth nor hostility, and they laid before him plainly what they sought and what they offered; he turned their terms over in silence, and at last found them worth accepting, and so the first door of that country opened to them. The second stood at the river-gate below the Crossing, where a chain and a customs officer barred the road, and they paid what was asked — somewhat more than the charter permitted, as both parties understood — and passed through without further argument. What remained was the country itself: autumn rains had churned the road to grey paste, the cold came early that year, and each morning offered the same quiet question of whether the miles ahead were worth the ones already spent behind them. They found, each morning, that the answer held; and when at last the familiar ridge-line rose above the treeline and the covenant's gates came into view, the party came in whole and the thing they had set out to do was done.
