The causeway ran out into the marsh on rotting pilings, and the water on both sides of it sat black and still in the hour before first light. The party had come from the east, where the reeds gave cover enough to move by, and they reached the old gatehouse without rousing the wardens who kept their fire on the far bank. One by one they went over the low wall at the gatehouse's shoulder — the stone was slick with moss and they moved slowly for it — and then they were inside, in the shadow of the passage that led down beneath the road.

They reached the lower vault unseen, and that was something.

The vault was not empty. Whatever had been left there to keep it was still keeping it: a thing that came at them from the far corner, heavy and low, and the fight that followed was close and ugly in the confined space. But they had known something would be there — they had pieced that much together from the warden's routines and the age of the lock above — and they had not let the darkness below the causeway talk them out of their purpose. They drove it back. When it was done they stood in the vault's damp air and listened to the water dripping somewhere in the walls.

The door at the vault's far end was iron-banded and old, set into the stone with a worked plate above its lintel. The plate bore markings, and this was why they had brought the one among them who knew old scripts — the one who had spent seasons in the covenant's library pulling apart water-damaged records from the first settlements in this marsh. He stood before it with his lamp and read what he could. What he could read told him very little. The markings were older than anything he had catalogued, their forms corrupt or simply unknown to him, and the few symbols he recognised gave him no confidence in the pattern. He tried two arrangements of what he thought he understood, pressing the symbols in the sequence the grammar seemed to demand. Nothing moved. The door gave no sign it had noticed him at all.

But the door was still shut, and they had not come this far to be turned by a door.

They put four hands to it — the two strongest of the party — and pulled. The iron bands were good iron; the hinges, however old, held. They shifted their grip and pushed instead, and when that failed they braced against the opposite wall and drove their shoulders into it in turns, and the door moved not at all. It was not merely locked; it was set into the stone in a way that made it one piece with the vault itself, and the force they could bring to bear against it was nothing to the force the stone was built to absorb. One of them split his brow where the impact was bad. They stopped.

They went back up the passage and over the wall in the grey light that was coming now over the marsh, and crossed the causeway while the wardens were changing watch, moving quickly and saying nothing. Whatever the vault held, it was still holding it.

The covenant sent four out in the last week of the cold season, when the marsh roads were still firm underfoot but the water had not yet risen to swallow them. Their quarry lay past the holding of the river-wardens — a drowned vault beneath the old causeway, which the wardens had claimed as their province and would not open to strangers.

The party's leader had spent three winters trading along the river before she took covenant work, and she went ahead to the wardens' hall alone and unarmed. She did not ask for passage. She sat with the warden-chief through a long meal and told him plainly what the covenant wanted and why, and she listened to what the wardens feared: that outsiders would pull what lay in the vault and not honor the old arrangement by which the wardens kept their toll on anything drawn up from it. She gave him the covenant's word on that arrangement, sealed in terms the chief could hold to, and by the time the fire burned low he had sent word to the gate-keeper to let them through.

Therefore they came to the causeway, and to the vault's iron door beneath it, where the water ran black around the stone lip. The gate-keeper was an old man who had worked that door for thirty years and trusted no arrangement made without his own hand in it. The covenant seal meant nothing to him. He wanted metal — what he considered fair for the trouble of opening the door, for sending his boy down to work the bilge-pump, for keeping the secret of who had come and gone. The party's purse-keeper counted out what was asked without argument, though it was more than half of what they had carried out. The old man pocketed it and pulled the door.

But the vault below was cold in a way that reached past wet and cold into something that settled in the chest and would not leave. They worked by torchlight for several hours, moving through water that came to their knees in the deeper passages. One man began to shake badly enough that his hands were useless. Another said they should go up. The leader would not go up. She had given her word to the warden-chief that this was a covenant matter, and she meant it to be finished. She stripped off her coat and gave it to the shaking man and told the other to keep moving, and they kept moving. They came up at last with what they had gone down for, and the old gate-keeper locked the door behind them without a word.

They were back in the covenant hall before the week was out.

They had crossed the wide marsh in the hours before dawn, when the mist sat thick enough to swallow a man at twenty paces. The plan was to pass the old drowned watchtower before the light came up — to be nothing more than a disturbance in the reeds, already gone. But the mud held them. One of the company stepped wrong in the dark and went down past the knee, and the sound of it carried, and the lantern on the watchtower swung their way.

The wardens came out with poles and dogs. The footing was too treacherous to run, so the party was herded to firmer ground and held there while the wardens decided what to make of them.

They had come this far for a reason: beyond the marsh lay a drowned vault beneath the old causeway, where something had been left when the waters rose three generations back. The wardens knew the vault. They did not let outsiders past.

One of the company stepped forward and offered what the party could not afford to lose — the silver they had brought for the return journey, and the tools they had hoped to carry back laden. She set them down in the mud without ceremony and told the elder warden that the covenant asked passage for this day only and would not come again without leave. It was not a plea. It was a plain accounting of what the party was worth and what it wanted, and she let the wardens weigh it.

The elder warden looked at the silver a long time. Then he told his people to stand aside.

They reached the vault by midmorning. When they turned for home that afternoon, the wardens showed them a longer road, drier, and did not stop them at the boundary. They came back carrying less than they had gone out with, and they came back with what they had gone to find.
