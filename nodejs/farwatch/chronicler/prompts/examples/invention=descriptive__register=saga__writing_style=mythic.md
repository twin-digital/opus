# Of the Company That Went Down to the Drowned Vaults

There came a season when the covenant sent a company into the marshlands east of the old causeway, where the waters had long since taken the lower halls of what was once a seat of considerable learning. The covenant's need was plain: within those sunken galleries lay the sealed repository of a school whose masters had not been heard from in three generations, and the covenant required what that school had known. The company went with this purpose, and with the understanding that the marsh-wardens who had claimed the ruins as their own would not welcome them.

They went in before first light, when the mist lay so thick upon the water that a man standing waist-deep was invisible to one standing at the bank. They knew the warden-paths, having spent the days prior watching from the reed-beds, and they moved through the outer works without so much as a ripple out of place. The wardens kept their fires and their low talk, and never knew a company had passed them. That much went well, and the company let themselves believe the rest would follow.

It did not.

They found the wardens had left a garrison in the first of the dry chambers — a guard-post wedged into what had been a scriptorium, the shelves long since stripped and the writing-desks turned into a barricade. The garrison was neither large nor particularly watchful, but it stood between the company and the passage downward, and there was no way around it. The company struck fast and from two directions at once, and the thing was over before the garrison had fully understood what had come upon them. The passage downward lay open.

Below, the water stood in the outer gallery to the knee, and the inner door — the great iron-banded threshold that sealed the repository proper — had not moved in all the years the marsh had held it. Around its frame, the builders had incised the conditions of its opening: a covenant of a different kind, written in the formal script of the old school, setting out exactly what must be given and spoken and done for the door to yield. The company had brought with them a man who had studied the old school's writings, who had given the covenant every reason to trust his learning. He knelt before those inscriptions for the better part of an hour, the cold water rising around him as the tide began to push in from the estuary, and at the end of it he rose and said that he could not be certain. The inscription was not corrupt; it was simply more particular than anything he had encountered, a dialect variant of the formal script that diverged at precisely the points that mattered. He gave them his best interpretation, and they followed it exactly, and the door did not open.

They were not men and women who surrendered a thing easily. They turned from the script to the door itself, to the hinges and the locking-bars and the stone of the frame, and they worked at it with everything they had brought for the purpose: pry-bars, wedges, rope and lever, the combined weight of bodies braced against the cold iron. The door held. The builders of that school had made the repository to outlast the school, and it had done so, and it continued to do so. The tide was coming in earnest by then, and the water in the gallery had reached the waist, and they had nothing left to bring to bear.

They came up out of the marsh the same way they had gone in, through the grey pre-dawn. The repository remained sealed behind them. What the old school had known, it continued to keep.

# Of the Expedition to the Drowned Margins

In the year when the covenant's title to the eastern reaches was yet unsettled, the elder council sent forth a company of three to treat with the marsh-wardens who held the causeway — for without the wardens' leave, no covenant party could pass into the flooded country beyond, and the covenant had need of what lay there.

They came to the wardens' hall at midday, when such men are sharpest and least inclined to charity. The wardens received them with the courtesy owed to strangers and the coldness owed to those whose business is not yet understood. The company's speaker — a woman who had served three covenants in her time and learned in each the art of speaking plainly to those who expect to be deceived — set out the covenant's claim without flourish: what it needed, why the wardens' interest and the covenant's interest were not opposed, and what cooperation between them would look like in the years to come. She spoke of old agreements between the margins and the upland houses, cited lineages the wardens had not expected her to know, and before the afternoon fire was lit the hall's mood had shifted from suspicion to something closer to recognition. The wardens opened the causeway.

Beyond the causeway the way grew difficult, and here the wealth of the covenant proved its worth. The inner channels of the drowned country are known only to the punt-men who have worked them since childhood, and without a punt-man the company would have spent a week finding what they sought or foundered in the reed-ways trying. The covenant's purse was not shallow, and the company did not haggle. They paid the figure asked by the eldest of the punt-men, added a further sum for his silence about the route, and thereby purchased not merely passage but expertise — the old man's knowledge of which channels held firm bottom and which were false, which stands of reed concealed open water and which were solid ground. The vault they had come to find lay two days into the margins; without him they would not have found it in a season.

What they discovered in the vault is recorded elsewhere. The third trial came not in the finding but in the return, when the rain that had threatened since their second morning finally broke and broke badly — three days of it, cold and driving, the punt-man's craft taking water faster than two of them could bail, the third working the pole through channels that had no bottom they could feel. The company had what they had come for. The shorter road back was flooded past navigating. The punt-man offered to put them ashore at a hamlet two hours distant and leave them to wait the rain out, and the warmth of a dry room and a fire pulled at them with real force.

They declined. The hamlet would have meant delay, and delay meant the causeway wardens hearing more than the covenant wished them to hear before the company returned. They bailed and poled, bailed and poled, and did not speak much of what the wet and the cold cost them. On the fourth morning the rain broke. They came through the causeway before noon and made the road home before dark.

The covenant received them and what they carried, and the eastern question was afterward settled more easily than it would otherwise have been. The wardens of the causeway sent word the following spring that they were willing to discuss a formal arrangement. The punt-man, it was said, retired before winter.

# Of the Expedition to the Drowned Vault

The company had crossed the causeway before the rains of the third week fully receded, when the flood still stood in the low fields and the reed-beds afforded cover enough that a careful party might reach the vault's outer works without drawing notice. This was their design: to move by night along the submerged margins of the old road, keeping to the shadow of the willows, trusting the marsh's silence to carry them through to the first gate unseen.

For a time the marsh was willing.

But the wardens who had kept that approach since the covenant's enemies first sealed the vault had spent generations learning to read the water's surface as others read a written page, and a line of disturbed reeds, a wake too deliberate against the still flood, spoke to them as plainly as a torch. The fires went up before the company reached the first gate-post. What had been intended as a passage through shadow became instead a passage in full sight, surrounded, and the company was brought to the outer threshold in the manner of those who are escorted rather than those who arrive.

That the wardens stopped at the threshold and went no further was not clemency; it was the limit of their authority. What lay beyond the outer gate was older than their commission, and it would answer only to its own terms.

The company therefore turned to reckoning what they carried that the vault's keeping might accept in place of the leave they had failed to take by stealth. The captain surrendered the covenant's seal — the pressed silver disc that had opened their credit across every waystation on the road, the token by which the covenant's voice spoke through them at distance. It was the nearest thing they carried to a claim of right, and the gate recognized it as such. The mechanism within — no warden's hand touched it — turned over, and the way opened.

They went in, and they came out again with what they had been sent to find.

The seal remained in the vault. They returned without it, and the covenant received both the gain and the loss in the same morning.
