# Farwatch — Target Saga (surface-first artifact)

*This is the **reading we want the game to produce**, authored by hand. It is a spec, not fiction
for its own sake: we build only the substrate needed to generate lines like these, at the
resolution these lines actually use. Each section is labelled with the player-facing **channel**
it represents. Outside-in, never inside-out.*

> Covenant: **The Tidebound.** Charter: *to wake the drowned god beneath the reef* (terminal,
> distant; demands exploration · magic · diplomacy). Two standing edicts inherited from your
> predecessor. One open thread inherited: *who drowned Aldous?*

---

## 1 — The opening read  · *[channel: opening / wonder]*

You come back to yourself slowly, the way the tide comes back. The Tidebound settle around your
regard like silt — and for a moment you are simply *here*, and they are glad of it, though most
could not say why.

What they want, you understand without being told: to wake the god that drowned beneath the reef,
in the age before the covenant had a name. It is the work of lifetimes, and they know it, and they
do it anyway. This is the shape of the thing you have been called to keep.

You come to know a few of them:

- **Maelis**, eldest, who keeps the rites and remembers what they cost. She has buried three
  patrons. She trusts you with nothing yet.
- **Gerald**, who dives, and who loves the deep water the way other men love the dry and living
  world. He is the best of them and the least careful.
- **Greta**, younger, who learned the deep from Gerald — and has not yet learned his fear of
  nothing.
- **Sered**, who keeps the stores and the count, and tells you the truth even when Maelis would
  rather he didn't.

There is a quarrel under the covenant's calm: the rites that would wake the god are Maelis's to
grant, and she will not grant the deep one. Gerald thinks her a coward. She thinks him a drowning
waiting for a date.

And there is an older silence no one will explain. Aldous, whom your predecessor loved, drowned the
winter before you came. No one says how. No one says it was an accident, either.

---

## 2 — What you said  · *[channel: your outputs — edicts & dispatch]*

You do not command much. You set down two things and leave the rest to them:

> **You declare:** *Protect the named above any prize. I would rather come back to fewer wonders
> and the same faces.*
>
> **You dispatch:** *Gerald, to chart the reef's deep shelf — as far as the cold light, and no
> farther.*

Then your regard sinks back, the way it always does, and the covenant runs on without you.

---

## 3 — The chronicle, on your return  · *[channel: the saga]*

You return on the turn of the season, and the chronicler — it is Sered now, since there is no one
else — tells you what the water did while you were gone.

Gerald went to the deep shelf, as you sent him. He reached the cold light, where you told him to
stop. And then he went on, because he has always gone on; because the deep water has never once
frightened him, and your law could not weigh more than that.

He found the bell. A great drowned bell of black iron, half-swallowed by the reef, and when the
current moved it, it rang — and Maelis, when she heard of it, went white and would not speak. It is
a sign. The god is down there, and nearer than the rites had ever dared to say. Your charter has
not, in living memory, been this close to true.

Greta went after him. Not for the bell — for *him*. She had learned the deep from Gerald, and she
had learned his recklessness with it, and when he did not turn back at the cold light, neither did
she. The shelf falls away into dark there. Gerald came back. Greta did not.

She was twenty-and-some, and named, and yours, and now she is a memorial and a grudge: Maelis has
not looked at Gerald since, and Gerald has not looked at the sea.

You set down a law to keep the named above the prize. The covenant brought you a prize, and one
fewer face.

---

## 4 — Asking why  · *[channel: conversation — the comprehension activity]*

You want to know why he went on. There is no panel that will tell you; you have to go and ask, and
what comes back is people, not answers.

*Gerald is mending a net that does not need mending.*

> "You want to know why." A long quiet. "The bell was ringing. You've never heard it — none of you
> have, not in lifetimes. I told myself I'd turn at the cold light. I meant to."

He does not mention Greta. He will not. That silence is the truest thing he says.

*Maelis, asked the same, does not look up from her rites.*

> "He has wanted to drown since the day he learned to swim. The deep is the only thing he ever loved
> without measure. You handed him an errand to the edge of it, patron — and you are surprised he
> stepped over."

*Sered, who keeps the count, only adds:*

> "Greta would have followed him into a fire. Everyone knew it but her."

Nothing here is a clean cause. But laid together — his love of the deep, the bell that called the
charter true, the girl who would follow him anywhere, and the law that pulled at him and lost — they
make a shape you can believe. You did not *read* why Gerald went on. You **worked it out**, from
people who each held a corner of it.

(And had Gerald drowned instead of Greta, this corner would have drowned with him. Some covenants
never learn why their worst day happened.)

---

## 5 — The adventure log  · *[opt-in mechanical lens — kept out of the chronicle]*

The chronicle carries no numbers; the story should not read like a ledger. But the machine is there,
and when you want to see it — to know the day was *fair*, and that your law was not just decoration —
you pull it up.

> **THE DEEP SHELF — the season's account**
> Gerald · *heed the dispatch-limit at the cold light* — DC 13
>   rolled **10**  (caution 3 · your edict *protect the named* **+4**) — **FAIL**, pressed on
> Gerald · *the deep dark* — DC 16
>   rolled **18**  (diving 7 · *loves the deep* **+4**) — **PASS** → the bell
> Greta · *followed Gerald* (bond: strong) · *the deep dark* — DC 16
>   rolled **9**  (diving 4) — **FAIL** → lost

There it is, and it is not arbitrary: your edict was a real weight on Gerald — **+4** toward turning
back. He failed the check by three. Your law did not *fail*; it was **outweighed**, this once, by a
man who loves the deep more than he fears anything. (You see the modifier; you do not see *why* his
love of the deep is so heavy — that is his whole buried history, and it stays opaque. *Expose the
dice; hide the genome.*)

And then the log shows you something the chronicle never did:

> **THE SHOAL WRECK — the same season, untold**
> Nerith · *take the silver past the reef-wall* — DC 12
>   rolled **15**  (caution 5 · your edict *protect the named* **+4**) — **HELD**, turned back; all home, hold empty

Nothing *happened* on the shoal, so no one told you. But the **same law**, on a steadier seeker,
quietly turned her back from a wreck full of silver and brought everyone home. The chronicle only
ever shows you the day your law broke. The log is where you find the days it held.

That was you.

---

## 6 — A report, degraded  · *[channel: diegetic state, mediated and fragile]*

Later you ask Sered for the count and the chart, the way you do.

The count he gives you plainly, because counting is his gift:

> *Forty-one of us, patron, give or take the ones too new to name. Eight you'd have known by face.
> Seven, now.*

The chart he gives you badly, because it was never his to keep:

> *The home water and the near reef I can show you true enough. The deep shelf I can't — Greta was
> the one who knew it, and she carried most of it in her head, not on the vellum. Past the cold
> light it's… I'd be guessing, patron. And I won't guess at a thing that's already taken one of us.*

So the deep shelf — the very place your charter now points — goes dark on the only map you have. To
see it again, you will need someone willing to go and learn it.

You know exactly who would volunteer.

---

## What this saga demands we build — and what it doesn't

Reading backward from the lines above, the **minimum substrate** that could generate them:

- **Seekers** with: a name; a *primary affinity* strong enough to **outweigh an edict** (Gerald
  loves the deep water); a *bond / lineage* edge (Greta learned the deep from Gerald); a *role*
  (Sered = quartermaster); a mortality state.
- **Edicts as numeric modifiers on specific checks** — ranked conditional biases the resolver
  applies to a roll (the edict was literally **+4** on Gerald's "heed" check), so they can be
  *outweighed* by a stronger affinity rather than nullified. This is what makes agency legible.
- **A resolver that records its checks** — DC, roll, and named modifiers per check — because the
  **opt-in adventure log** is just a view of that record. Clean chronicle, raw log underneath.
- **A dispatch** carrying a parameter that can be exceeded ("to the cold light, no farther").
- **A causal graph** as the *engine's* ground truth: every meaningful change logs its *because*-edges,
  terminating in axiomatic facts ("the oldest thing recorded of him"). The player never reads it
  directly — **comprehension is diegetic**: you assemble the *why* from fallible, biased **testimony**
  (Gerald evades, Maelis is bitter, Sered is plain) and clues. The graph keeps the testimonies
  consistent and the LLM honest (no node → no claim); and a cause can be **buried** if everyone who
  knew it dies.
- **Permanence**: death removes a seeker and converts them into a *memorial + grudge* — a lasting
  relationship change (Maelis ↛ Gerald), not a deleted row.
- **Minted flavor** from the event, stored once ("Gerald has not looked at the sea").
- **Role-mediated, decaying knowledge**: a quartermaster's count (accurate) vs. a map that *goes
  dark* because the seeker who held that knowledge died. Knowledge is attached to people.
- **Sifter salience** the thread already exercises: a named character, a permanence (death), a
  *tested edict*, a *reversal* (prize won / face lost), an *open thread* (Aldous still unanswered; a
  new one opened — the bell, the god). **But** the sifter surfaces *exceptions* (defiance is
  story-worthy; obedience isn't), so the chronicle structurally **understates** the player's agency.
  The **log is the corrective** — it shows the quiet quests where the law *held* (Nerith turned back),
  which the chronicle never narrates. "That was you" lives in the log, not the saga.

What it conspicuously **did not need**: a 12-dimensional temperament vector, five themes, a large
vocabulary. The whole saga is carried by **~2 salient traits, 1 bond, 1 edict, 1 causal chain, and
1 death.** That is the concrete proof of "match substrate resolution to the channel" — the genome
can start *tiny*, and grow only where a future reading actually needs more.
